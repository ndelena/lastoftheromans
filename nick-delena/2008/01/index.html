Old habits die hard

By
Nick DeLena
on January 31, 2008 10:36 PM | Permalink | Comments (0) | TrackBacks (0)

Ever-turbulent Italy has once again vomited its most recent government of Romano Prodi (pictured) in a no confidence vote. This represents the collapse of Italy's 61st government since the end of the 2nd World War.

As much as we may gripe about the exclusion and single-mindedness of the American political parties, Italy is much the opposite. At current there are some 39 political parties holding seats in the Camera dei Deputati. They range from communists to xenophobes. Each government must set up delicate coalitions in order to create a governable majority. Prodi's was rooted in the center-left but involved communists. I believe his government was well intentioned but ultimately lacked the boldness needed to pass crucial reforms.

Today Italy lags as the slowest growing member of the G8. According to some economists, Spain has now passed it in terms of GDP per capita. The south, not-so-affectionately referred to as the Mezzogiorno, is quite nearly another country; much poorer, much more rural, and with much more unemployment. Naples is awash in garbage; unions have refused to collect any additional trash because the Campanian dumps are overflowing. The EU is currently threatening Italy with penalties if it doesn't sort out the potential health disaster. Leadership is needed.

What isn't needed, however, is a return of former Prime Minister Silvio Berlusconi. He's made remarks lately that indicate he wants to assume his old office a mere 20 months after being voted out. He spent the majority of his five year tenure making legal maneuvers to keep him from being prosecuted, neglecting the country's needed economic reforms. Italy today is one of the developed world's most heavily regulated economies. Buona fortuna, Italia, you need it.

Election '08: Quo vadis

By
Nick DeLena
on January 9, 2008 5:23 PM | Permalink | Comments (0) | TrackBacks (0)
I consider myself a pretty decisive person, but when it comes to this election I am having a hard time deciding among the candidates. I supported Joe Biden, the Democratic Senator from Delaware because I thought he had the best blend of pragmatism and experience. He didn't make it past Iowa and judging from the caucus results you'd wonder if he was even in Iowa. I suppose this election just catches me at a philosophical fork-in-the-road; I find myself becoming more and more alienated from the positions and behavior of the Democratic Party. Make no mistake, however, this doesn't also mean that I'm moving closer and closer to the Republican Party - trust me, far from that. I'm not sure where this puts me, personally it feels like I'm just moving off into the tall grass.

I've always been fiscally-oriented when it comes to politics; my personal perspective is that social issues should really be outside of the domain of legislators. Call me a Libertarian, but the Constitution didn't entrust Congress with the responsibility for deciding who can marry who or what type of family is best, or what religion I should subscribe to. I just want Congress to run the country and do it as efficiently as possible. That includes fixing Iraq, cutting waste and spending, and defending the borders and fixing immigration.

Currently there is no candidate that has any sort of record as a waste-cutter, who is detached enough from the party bases that could govern effectively, who would stick to matters of Government (and not personal matters), while at the same time improving our standing in the world and righting the federal budget.

It seems as well that this malaise that has paralyzed government extends to some extent to the state and local levels. I can tell you from experience that the near $5,000 a year I pay in property taxes seems to buy me only really expensive garbagemen and police who get their cars stolen from in front of the police station. I think I want a refund.
