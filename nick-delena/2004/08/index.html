A Curious row

By
Nick DeLena
on August 28, 2004 3:13 PM | Permalink | Comments (0) | TrackBacks (0)

'Tis a very strange time indeed when Britain's Tories are shunned by Republicans, yet that is what has happened to Tory leader Michael Howard by none other than the Commissar himself, Karl Rove. Howard was allegedly told by Rove he'll never meet President Bush: "You can forget about meeting the president full stop. Don't bother coming." Those comments were in response to the criticism Howard leveled against Tony Blair over the mishandling of Iraq intelligence.

There are several peculiar elements to this. Several. Historically, Republicans and Tories are bed buddies (see Reagan & Thatcher), but now we have a Labour and Republican marriage. Two parties that could not disagree more on policies (Labour is somewhat to the left of our Democrats) have found one militaristic common footing. The Tories' conservative thunder has been stolen by Labour, leaving them to "oppose" Blair by saying things like "We would have still supported going to war by backing a differently worded motion." What great opposition indeed. The greater problem here is that when you have two major political parties swaping positions and doing a two-step like this, democracy takes a back seat. Thankfully for the British, there is still one major party that they can turn to come next elections, the Liberal Democrats. With a recent by-election win in Leicester South, it now seems set that the Lib Dems will overtake the Tories, leavi
ng the largest two parties in Britain the left, and the lefter. Wouldn't that be nice here?

A note from the past

By
Nick DeLena
on August 23, 2004 12:55 AM | Permalink | Comments (3) | TrackBacks (0)

July 26, 1915: "In this war, which obliges one to concentrate oneís strength to the utmost, I feel that I have lost many of the treasures amassed during a period of gradual auspicious development in time of peace. The knowledge which I acquired at school and at the university, the interests aroused in me by a civilian occupation, are lost to sight and memory, and only with difficulty will it be possible to regain them. You know that having been too early forced to take a serious view of life, my youth was short; I could not even fall in love properly; but it is this awful war which has made an old man of me. Certainly here at the front my body has become weather-proof and my muscles as hard as iron, but my mind has not developed. Everybody who looks daily into the cold eye of death and gazes on so many dead faces bearing the stamp of suffering and renunciation, becomes certainly callous, but also old, very very old."

Kurt Rohrbach, German soldier, killed October 6, 1916.

"Afghanistan could implode"

By
Nick DeLena
on August 4, 2004 11:27 AM | Permalink | Comments (4) | TrackBacks (0)

A very critical report of the coalition's handling of the Afghan reconstruction was released on July 29 by a British Parliamentary Committee. Tory Sir John Stanley says Afghanistan is on a "knife-edge" and could "implode" without more troops, something Afghan PM Hamid Karzai has been requesting for two years. With American attention on Iraq for two years now, Afghanistan has fallen into a nether category of forgotten reconstruction projects, while opium, the production of which the Taleban had eliminated, has spiked to such an extent that 90% of all the heroin on British streets is from Afghanistan.

Once again, through DoD mismanagement and Iraq preoccupation we are running the risk of returning Afghanistan to the chaos we attempted to rescue it from. Can we really afford to have Afghanistan slip back into anarchy and warlord control?
