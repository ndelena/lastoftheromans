Thermopylae, LA

By
Nick DeLena
on September 13, 2005 5:16 PM | Permalink | Comments (0) | TrackBacks (0)

I don't have a lot to say about the tragedy in New Orleans. The Economist's lead article is called "The Shaming of America" and reads something like: "The dreadful aftermath of Hurricane Katrina has exposed personal and structural failings in America's government, and eroded respect for the country around the world..." I don't go so far as that. But I do think hurricane Katrina took us face to face with our own third world: the poor, urban black South. Nearly all of the faces of the afflicted seen in disaster footage were black. Lacking the resources to escape the hurricane, they had no choice but to remain.

The aftermath, however, was truly post-apocalyptic; guns were among the first items looted at local Wal-Marts, along with televisions and electronics. Buildings were defaced. Fires set. Gangs of heavily armed young men roamed the city stealing and raping women. Nearly the entire population of the city was holed up in the superdome, with no running water, drastically low on supplies, and falling victim to the armed gangs. State and Federal officials were nowhere in sight.

It really proves that man (or maybe just American man) will almost immediately revert to barbarism and chaos once the machinations of law disappear. I've been unable to confirm it, but I heard a story of an interview with an Indonesian tsunami survivor who asked something to the effect of "Who really is the third world country? When we were hit by the tsunami we helped each other, we didn't steal and rape each other." It goes to show that wealth isn't only financial.

Also in the aftermath, many of the police of New Orleans either disappeared or failed to show up for duty, leaving only at one point about two hundred men to defend the entire city. In an interview several days later, the police chief compared their stand with that of the Greek Spartans at the Battle of Thermopylae in 480 BC. Facing an onslaught of an army forty times more numerous, the ancient Greeks used the narrow pass at Thermopylae as a way of forcing the massive Persian army into a bottleneck. However, a Greek traitor gave the Persians knowledge of an alternate path. The Greek forces, from all cities, numbering some 7,000 were sent away by their leader, Spartan king Leonidas. Interestingly, 600 Thespians under their leader Demophilus refused to leave. What is interesting is that the Thespians, unlike the militaristic Spartans, were citizen-soldiers. Demophilus made his living as an architect. The act so honored the Spartans that it is said they exchanged cloaks with the Thespians and promised to be allies for eternity. Leonidas and Demophilus remained at Thermopylae with 900 men, facing an army that could have been as large as 200,000. Though they fell, the gave enough time for the Greek army to disburse to the cities to defend them, eventually allowing the Greeks to defeat the Persians at the Battle of Salamis. And truly, in a sense the overwhelming Persian forces make a good metaphor for the floods and looting and the challenge faced by anyone who has any regard for law and order.
