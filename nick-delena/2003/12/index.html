the privilege of the disconnect

By
Nick DeLena
on December 15, 2003 12:12 AM | Permalink | Comments (0)

BAGHDAD, Iraq (CNN) -- For a man who killed tens of thousands of people and tortured his enemies, Saddam Hussein was taken into custody in a surprisingly peaceful manner, Defense Secretary Donald Rumsfeld said Sunday night on CBS' "60 Minutes."

Hussein captured. This is probably the only positive news I have heard regarding Iraq in... a long time. Maybe ever. I just hope now that we relinquish him to either an Iraqi war crimes tribunal or the International Criminal Court (unlikely given the U.S.'s position on that organization). Hussein must be brought to justice over his crimes against the Kurds, the Shi'ites, the Kuwaitis, the Iranians, etc. Finally the potential for some kind of delivery of justice has presented itself. Time will tell if the United States allows it to happen to its former pal.

Winter here in Boston has come without a star. We're in the second sizable snow storm in as many weeks. Six or so inches this time. Better than 25" like last week. The city is quieter, but unimpressed. News comes down the pipe that Suffolk University Students for Peace and Justice are hosting a forum/debate in February featuring Howard Zinn and B.U. President John Silbur at the C. Walsh Theatre. I'm there.

Ramsey Clark: "It has never happened in history that a nation that has won a war has been held accountable for atrocities committed in preparing for and waging that war."

Oh, humanity, you whore.

Decline and fall (of production)

By
Nick DeLena
on December 7, 2003 2:41 PM | Permalink | Comments (1) | TrackBacks (0)

Via Jim via Kottke.org via AlterNet.org: a dual book review article concerning the amount of time left before the world runs out of oil.

Marion King Hubbert, a geophysicist who spent his career in field work for Shell Oil became widely known for his model on production peaks and declines in oil production. The theory became known as "Hubbert's Peak."

Hubbert poured his own most productive years into directing research for Shell Oil, where he knew that the discovery of new U.S. oilfields had peaked in the 1930s. Hubbert factored this data into his bell-curve model, and predicted in 1956 that production of crude oil in the contiguous 48 states would peak sometime between 1966 and 1972. The oil industry dismissed his prediction and discredited his work.

U.S. crude oil production peaked in 1970, and has fallen steeply ever since.

Hubbert became a legend, and his prediction became known as "Hubbert's Peak." In the past decade ñ as the rate of discovery of worldwide oil reserves has slowed to a trickle ñ a flood of Hubbert followers have tackled the obvious question: When will global production peak?

An expostulation...

By
Nick DeLena
on December 1, 2003 11:21 AM | Permalink | Comments (3)

At the risk of sounding frivolous and petty, I must protest that I received an egg nog latte this morning after I clearly ordered a soy vanilla latte.

I stepped to the register: "grande soy vanilla latte please."

The cashier acknowledged and repeated my order to the man working the espresso bar, who wrote the correct order on the little boxes on the cup.

He then proceeded to make an egg nog latte, while "soy vanilla latte" stared at him from the cup.

Ok. I am really trying hard not to be one of those guys who flies off the handle when their minestrone has one basil leaf too many, or if their tomato bisque is more than 65% tomato... but at $3.90 a coffee I'd prefer to pay for what I order, rather than this rejectamenta that tastes like cookie batter and lard. AND it was made with milk, not soy milk. This is atrocious.

To make matters worse, I found out this morning that my web design domain netmodus.com had expired and was immediately purchased by a third-party. For some reason my domain registrar, DROA decided not to inform me of the expiration. Could things get worse today? Probably.
