cruising

By
Nick DeLena
on April 26, 2004 4:14 PM | Permalink | Comments (6) | TrackBacks (0)

totally mentally ripped
driving the streets in the afternoon
stopping at signals
looking at people walking on the sidewalks
it's reality but a reality that has
faded away.
you know your judgement is
warped but you don't give a
damn.
too long on earth, that's it,
that's your problem.
a story told too often.

the signal changes and you
cross the avenue
enter a side street
drive along
the houses are small and
sad, have no heart.
the asphalt boils under
your wheels
and there's no place to go,
no surprise, no wonderment.

too long on earth, old dog,
you are dirtied with life.

you circle back to the avenue,
park behind a taco stand,
get out, walk to the counter,
wait.

a heavy girl approaches.
she stands, looks at you.
you pretend to be composed.
coffee, you say, small, black.

she smiles at you.
the smile says:
i know you're crazy, but it's alright.

you get the coffee,
take it back to the car.
you are facing a dirty dark
yellow wall.
you sip the coffee:
bitter horrible swill.

you drink half a cup, dump it out the window,
back out of there
and then you're driving west.

a psychiatrist wont help,
a psychologist wont help,
a god wont help.
drinking's old and drugs make it worse.

you just drive in your car
for a hundred years.
for centuries.
your head ripped
rising up through
the sunroof
lolling on a long snake neck
smiling a bloody smile,

paradise at last.

-Bukowski's "Cruising"

Un mare di risate.

By
Nick DeLena
on April 19, 2004 12:50 AM | Permalink | Comments (2) | TrackBacks (0)

Back in September I posted about an Ellis Island record I found regarding my paternal great grandfather's immigration to the U.S. and my ongoing attempts to acquire dual Italian-U.S. citizenship. The record I initially found turned out to be of my great-great uncle Gennara, who in turn settled in Mount Vernon, New York and started a New York/New Jersey branch of the DeLenas (DiLenas).

My true great grandfather's record was harder to find, thanks to t
he slow-witted Ellis Island employees who screwed up the spelling of my great grandfather's home town of Afragola, spelling it "Apagola." His information is as follows:

Name: DiLena, Giuseppe
Ethnicity: Italian
Place of Residence: Apagola, Italy (should read Afragola)
Date of Arrival: May 17, 1907
Age on Arrival: 23
Gender: Male
Marital Status: Single
Ship of travel: Madonna
Port of Departure: Naples, Campania, Italy

Interestingly, after I posted the original September 25th, 2003 piece, someone from Afragola came across it (I'm not quite sure how, as I am not a prominent result on any google searches for Italian ancestry related to Naples, Campania or Afragola). On this message board, the man posted (in Italian), regarding what he read on my site as well as the comments between me and Chris that followed the original post.

The April axiom

By
Nick DeLena
on April 11, 2004 10:25 PM | Permalink | Comments (0) | TrackBacks (0)

There are a few new developments of late, nothing overwhelmingly significant. I purchased a '94 Volkswagen Jetta GLS a few weeks ago. It was purchased hit and is getting fixed as we speak. Quick stats: 5spd, 100k mi, sunroof, power windows/locks, Sony CDX-F7700 mp3/xm/cd player.

I have gotten new netModus business cards as I'm gearing up my web design business.

I won $60 playing poker Wednesday.

In yet another example of my notoriously mundane dreams (and speaking of business cards), last night I dreamt that all the business cards I keep stored in my organizer disappeared. That's it; that's the extent of my dream. The business cards were gone. End of dream. Is my imagination gone? Is that the best my subconscious can conjur? I hope it's because I'm just so damn creative when awake that sleep brings rest to my subconscious as well (blatant lies, blatant lies).

I spent some time with one of the exes this weekend. I had a great time, for good or ill. All my lovers were there with me, all my past and futures, and we all went to heaven in a little row boat. There was nothing to fear and nothing to doubt... If only things were so poetically ideal.


Old songs stay till the end. Sad songs remind me of friends. And the way it is, I could leave it all.

'Our home and native land'

By
Nick DeLena
on April 5, 2004 5:01 PM | Permalink | Comments (5) | TrackBacks (0)

Joanna Harmon is considering whether to leave the United States for Canada. Nik and Nancy Phelps practically have visas in hand to set up business in Belgium. Joan Magit and her husband are eyeing Vancouver. Amy Gertz moved to the United States from Canada two and a half years ago ñ she's now moving back.

These are scary times for progressive Americans. Many feel isolated and embarrassed by the actions of our government. Some are downright terrified at what will happen if Bush is re-elected in November. When politics get too bleak, it's comforting to remind ourselves that we can always move to another country. But how many of us are serious about it?

A clip of an interesting article on AlterNet.org, "Flight Not Fight" focusing on Americans who've decided to move to Canada based on personal politics. For a while friends and I have joked about and experimented with it (Madrid, London) but not actually done it.

I remember a Washington Post article from last year on Canada's emerging identity as a leading progressive nation. The article, in effect, implied Canada is becoming more American than America.

"What emerges," writes Toronto-based author and pollster Michael Adams, "is a portrait of two nations evolving in unexpected directions: The once shy and deferential Canadians, who used to wait to be told by their betters what to do and how to think, have become more skeptical of traditional authority and more confident about their own personal decisions and informal arrangements. Americans, by contrast, seeking a little of the 'peace and order' that Canadians hoped 'good government' would provide, seem inclined to latch on to traditional institutional practices, beliefs, and norms as anchors in a national environment that is more intensely competitive, chaotic, and even violent."

Adams and other scholars point to the varying influence of religion in the two societies. Two-thirds of Americans think religion is important, while a third of Canadians do, according to polls. Nearly half of Americans say they attend church weekly, compared with one in five Canadians.

"We don't have Pat Buchanans and we don't have powerful religious movements shaping social po
licy the way you do," says Neil Nevitte, professor of political science at the University of Toronto, who also has measured national values.

American national values these days seem to be nationalism, disdain for human rights, militarism, religious intolerance and corporate corruption. Is this the America we've inherited?

Is running away from a fight the right thing to do? Wouldn't that just be one less vote for those with similar views?

I don't know. I look at politics today and see no one representing what I believe. And I see myself more and more alone in my beliefs. I always have liked Second Cup coffee.
