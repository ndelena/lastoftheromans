Un mare di risate.

By
Nick DeLena
on April 19, 2004 12:50 AM | Permalink | Comments (2) | TrackBacks (0)

Back in September I posted about an Ellis Island record I found regarding my paternal great grandfather's immigration to the U.S. and my ongoing attempts to acquire dual Italian-U.S. citizenship. The record I initially found turned out to be of my great-great uncle Gennara, who in turn settled in Mount Vernon, New York and started a New York/New Jersey branch of the DeLenas (DiLenas).

My true great grandfather's record was harder to find, thanks to t
he slow-witted Ellis Island employees who screwed up the spelling of my great grandfather's home town of Afragola, spelling it "Apagola." His information is as follows:

Name: DiLena, Giuseppe
Ethnicity: Italian
Place of Residence: Apagola, Italy (should read Afragola)
Date of Arrival: May 17, 1907
Age on Arrival: 23
Gender: Male
Marital Status: Single
Ship of travel: Madonna
Port of Departure: Naples, Campania, Italy

Interestingly, after I posted the original September 25th, 2003 piece, someone from Afragola came across it (I'm not quite sure how, as I am not a prominent result on any google searches for Italian ancestry related to Naples, Campania or Afragola). On this message board, the man posted (in Italian), regarding what he read on my site as well as the comments between me and Chris that followed the original post.

The translation (with thanks to Chris):

"Certainly there is no shortage of individuals who are convinced that their ancestors from Afragola were named Daniella (Daniela), Cayetano (Gaetano), Victorio (Vittoria), Julia (Giulia), had surnames such as DiPaolo (di Palo), and who were born in places like St. Giuseppe Vesuviano (San Giuseppe Vesuviano), Pomiglarco (Pomigliano d'Arco), Montercozvicro Ravello (Montecorvino Rovella), Amolfi (Amalfi), Grumo App. (Grumo Nevano suppongo) and others which I am not even able to decipher, like Willavosse, or Sant'Angelo Wasavello: now, apparently there is a fellow who is quite convinced that his grandfatherís name had been Gennara DiLena (surely, it would have been Gennara di Lena, what with all of the homonyms that exist in the townÖ) and that Afragola is found in the central part of Italy. This guy is doing some research on his (recent) origins as a result of his desire to obtain Italian citizenship, and has consequently discovered that the reason he is called DeLena is that his grandfather, after emigrating (while he was still a minor--in 1905 in Italy and even now in many states in the USA one is considered an adult at the age of 21, and he was 18) had gained this small, innocent variation to the surname (in America you could have it changed in five minutes, but certainly whoever changed it was largely ignorant because the variation in vocalization, di, would have to be written ìde Lenaî and not ìDeLenaî.) In the comment section of the guestbook, absurd responses are made: that it is a name (itís not clear if heís talking about his grandfatherís name or the name of the town) indicative of Camorristi (the author of the post claims that a travel guide in his possession recognizes it as pertaining to the ìtriangle of deathî of the Camorra); that Afragola is a very pretty name because it means ìto strawberry,î and that it would be admirable if he changed his surname to its original form."

Now, it is somewhat apparent that the person who posted this is not entirely fluent in English (perfectly excusable as I am much less than anything near fluent in Italian). The name Afragola is not indicative of the Camorra, rather the region is, according to my travel guide, "Italy, The Rough Guide" by Ros Belford, Martin Dunford and Celia Woolfrey, published by the Penguin Group and originally purchased by me in 1999 in Spain (in the Puerta de Callao, if I recall).

The passage reads:
"North of Naples: Caserta, C·pua and around

There's not much to draw you into the territory immediately north of Naples. The towns just outside the city - Casoria, Afragola, Acerra - are collectively known as the "Triangle of death" for their Camorra connections and make up a bleak conturbation of poor housing and industrial messiness."

Unfortunately that is the first and last mention of Afragola in any context in this guide. Now, judging by their interpretation of my post, they were not happy. And understandably so; no one wants to hear their home referred to as a shithole. I meant no harm by it and still plan on seeing and judging for myself someday. Soon, hopefully.

For any interested Afragolesi that may be browsing through, Giuseppe DiLena (di Lena) was the son of Francesco DiLena and Caterina Russo, and Giuseppe married Teresa Quintino (daughter of Luigi Quintino and Rose Castaldo). Teresa was also Afragolese. Giuseppe's parents are believed to have remained in Afragola.

Another user of the Afragola message board responded to this comment with: mi sono fatta un mare di risate! or "I had myself a sea of laughter (after reading that post)."

Interestingly, after a bit of investigation, the girl, named, Giusy who had herself this mare di risate has a website of her own. If you can tolerate the advertisments there are some beautiful pictures in the "foto" section. What I want to know is, when is their next beer and nutella party and am I invited? (And is her friend going to be there too?)
