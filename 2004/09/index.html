The question of "wealth"

By
Nick DeLena
on September 14, 2004 2:47 PM | Permalink | Comments (14) | TrackBacks (0)

In yet another example of the injustices inherent in our corporate interests-dominated healthcare industry, the BBC News' latest Photo journal titled "America's uninsured" features a story about Defannie Davis, whose husband Roy suffered a massive stroke in 2002, as a result of not being able to afford his blood pressure medication. After his stroke, his kidneys failed, he contracted pneumonia and he was in a coma for three days. He's now unable to speak at all and is undergoing various forms of therapy. After his initial stroke he suffered another one, and a heart attack. Only after incurring immense medical expenses did Medicaid kick in. We are not told what the Davis family owes in outstanding medical costs... we're only told that one bill alone was over $26,000. Under Medicaid, the Davis family only pays $1 for their medication. Roy Davis must undergo dialysis three time a week and has developed numerous infections as a result of the dialysis procedure.

It is a journal that seems to touch upon the overwhelming inhumanity and irony of 45 million Americans going uninsured - in the "wealthiest" nation on earth. Our current system demands the poor be poorer before it will offer assistance. In both my stays in the U.K. and Spain all health care and medication was freely available (even to me). To the European viewers of the photo journal, the Davis' story must seem barbaric. And it is.

Assault Weapons Ban Expiry

By
Nick DeLena
on September 13, 2004 4:25 PM | Permalink | Comments (11) | TrackBacks (0)

At midnight tonight, September 13, 2004, the 1994 Brady Bill banning assault weapons will expire. In just seven hours from the time of this post, AK-47s, UZIs, TEC-9s, flash suppressors and bayonetted rifles will all be perfectly legal from Maine to the Mexican border.

President Bush, who claims he supports an extension of the ban, has not urged Congress to extend it, instead coming out in criticism of John Kerry, who recently advocated for the ban's extension.

Here are some images of the weapons that will soon be legal:

   The Kalashnikov AK-105

   The UZI


   The Beretta SC70/90


   The AR-15


   The Steyr AUG


   The Striker 12

The last one is a particularly lethal shotgun known as the Striker 12; the round barrel underneath is a revolving chamber, making the gun automatic firing. Yes, an automatic shotgun.

In gathering research about the bill, I found a site called AWbansunset. Their motto is "Fighting for the expiration of the '94 "Assault Weapons" Ban!"

They have a link titled "Why Would Anyone Want to Own [an assault weapon]?" in which they say: "There are many answers to that question. Itís kind of like asking a car enthusiast why they would ever want to own a 425 horsepower 1968 Corvette Stingray. There are many reasons, some objective, some emotional. All of them legitimate, at least to the driver."

I realize that both a 1968 Corvette and an AK-105 automatic machine gun can easily kill a crowd of people, but at least the former was designed for an alternate purpose.

They continue to say that military rifles are "easy to operate", "reliable", "fun to shoot" and "very accurate." The lynchpin of their argument is in "personal defense" matters. I could not paraphrase it more to fit my argument, so I will quote it directly:

With the right type of ammunition, military style rifles can be a very effective defensive weapon. Many police agencies have recognized their value and have adopted their use in close quarter tactical operations. In these post 9/11 days, prudent Americans recognize that we are all vulnerable and should be prepared to defend ourselves, our families, our communities, and our country from threats that can strike us anytime and anyplace. A well-trained person, armed with a military style rifle can provide a deterrent and, if necessary, an effective defense. Full capacity magazines, whether for rifles or pistols, are also very important in this role, particularly in a situation where a homeowner finds him/herself facing multiple attackers.

Are people really that paranoid? Regardless, their argument that police forces have adopted these types of weapons does not hold water. In 1997, police in a North Hollywood, CA, shootout with two men had to pull back, because the men were armed with assault weapons. After the event, Jim Pasco, executive director of the Fraternal Order of Police said: "An AK-47 fires a military round. In a conventional home with dry-wall walls, I wouldn't be surprised if it went through six of them...Police are armed with weapons that are effective with criminals in line of sight. They don't want and don't need weapons that would harm innocent bystanders." It's that simple.

Also, one of the most notorious uses of assault weapons was the 1993 Branch-Davidian standoff in Waco, Texas. According to a federal affadavit, Davidians had amassed 123 AR-15s, 44 AK-47s, 2 Barrett .50 calibers, 2 Street Sweepers, an unknown number of MAC-10 and MAC-11s, 20 100-round drum magazines, and 260 large-capacity banana clips. "The weapons were bought legally from gun dealers and at gun shows." 74 men, women and children died.

I don't understand why this is even an issue. The 1994 ban should have been made permanent, without a sunset clause. No hunter needs an assault weapon to kill an animal. (Arguably, no hunter needs to hunt either. I hear sup
ermarkets are well stocked these days.)

This is a shame.

"The world's best hope..."

By
Nick DeLena
on September 11, 2004 2:53 PM | Permalink | Comments (0) | TrackBacks (0)

hree years ago, I sat on the subway at Wonderland station, waiting for the train to leave. It was my junior year of college and I was late for a marketing class. It was early but warm and I was groggy and disgruntled as usual. Typically the train waits for no more than five minutes, but this particular morning I sat for fifteen or twenty minutes. The driver came on and said that downtown service had been suspended. He ran out of the front of the car and into the station chief's office. I caught a glimpse of three people inside huddled around a television before the door shut.

I drove back home to find three messages on my answering machine from Tom. The first one was just to say that something bizarre happened and a plane hit the World Trade Center. I figured it was a small propellered plane horribly off course or in danger. The second message was markedly more frantic, as the second plane hit. I hit stop before the message finished, grabbed my phone and turned the television on. I was on the phone with Tom for a few hours - we were both silent; watching the television in shock. They broke from New York to Washington as Peter Jennings on ABC first reported smoke sighted and I remember my first thought was if Boston would be next.

My house is only about four miles from Logan airport in Boston, and my neighborhood is below the flight path for two or three of the runways. There's always the remote sound or sight of an airplane somewhere. That afternoon, for the first time, I went outside and down to the beach at the end of the street and heard nothing. There was no activity at the airport and the skies were blue and quiet. I sat there for an hour or so, and soon saw two F-15 fighter jets scream along the ocean heading south and turning over Boston. It seemed quite unnatural to see our military on duty- at home. For the next three days the F-15s were the only objects in the sky, steely gray and reflective in the sunlight.

In the days that followed, America had a historic opportunity to take the world's unity to heart and fight a "war on terror" with hundreds of allies and trillions of dollars in resources - and with billions of people behind the cause. This strategy was renounced quickly, with the administration instead opting for a rally-behind-the-dictator style war frenzy. Osama was the objective, then he was not. al-Qaeda was the objective, then it was not. Afghanistan was the battleground, then it was forgotten. Now we're embroiled in an Iraqi occupation with hundreds of thousands of troops and hundreds of billions of tax dollars. And now there's talk of Syria and Iran being next.

Yes, it is three years later.

And what do we see? A slipping, a loss of control, a distortion in the world's balance. Allies renounced, American military bases spanning massive swaths of formerly Soviet territory, cutting into previously unknowable countries such as Kyrgizstan and Tajikistan.

An administration eager to Americanize Iraq, at the cost of 1,000+ American lives (over 7,000 injured), and tens of thousands of Iraqi lives. An administration that chooses an imperial war and turns its back on legitimate humanitarian crises as the one occuring in Darfur.

The USA Patriot Act shoved through congress overnight, allowing government searches without warrants. Detentions without due process. A complete disregard for habeas corpus.

The neo-conservatives have won the civil war in the Republican party. The old fiscal conservatives who were reluctant to send the military anywhere are dead and gone. We now witness the party of the zealous evangelicals bent on simplifying a complex world. They are actively fighting to undermine diversity and harmony at home and hasten an ideological reshaping of the world in their own narrow and distorted image. They spend deeply into deficit, while distributing subsidies to the wealthiest 1%. Their Iraq experiment has gone horribly awry; pushing moderate, reform-minded Arabs into anti-American positions, undermining democratic reforms in Syria, Iran and Egypt, sending a message to the world that the United States is not interested in home-grown democracy, but in "Made in the USA"-branded socio-corporatism on the sharp end of a bayonet.

We've now given our once-No.1 enemy (al-Qaeda, remember them?) a historic opportunity to massacre as many American soldiers as they can in Iraq, as our troops are kept there longer and longer, as retired military personnel are called back, as soldiers are denied leave and not told when they'll return home to their loved ones. (As Donald Rumsfeld realizes American military might is finite.)

We see the politicization of our national tragedy at the Republican National Convention. We see 3,000 people - Americans, Brits, Australians, Indians, Christians, Athiests, Jews, Muslims, Hindus, Sikhs, like you or I who lost their lives simply by going to work made into fear-driven stump speeches. When our heart-attack prone bleeding from the mouth Vice President tells the country ""It's absolutely essential that eight weeks from today, on Nov. 2, we make the right choice, because if we make the wrong choice then the danger is that we'll get hit again and we'll be hit in a way that will be devastating from the standpoint of the United States." - vote George Bush or die.

Ours is an America hijacked, careening down a dead-end mountain road of cliff-edges. We need to wrest control of the emergency brake and take back our democracy, not just for ourselves, but for the world.

The military and police response is necessary and the networks inspired by Bin Laden have fortunately taken as many blows as they have delivered. But al-Qaeda has not disappeared, and the situation in Afghanistan and Iraq is playing into the hands of the jihadists. In the flood of reporting following that warm September day, I heard one commentator say that the United States was "the world's best hope for ordinary days." I believed it then, and even after three years of chaos, war and decline I still believe it.

RNC Week: The Final Analysis

By

on September 5, 2004 9:29 PM | Permalink | Comments (0) | TrackBacks (0)

With the end of the week comes the end of the Republican National Convention's "All 9/11, all the time" God-festival at Madison Square Garden. Between bouts of Elizabeth Dole saying "Freedom of religion is not freedom from religion" (how can freedom be conditional?) and Schwarzenegger's "If you have a pulse, you're a Republican" diatribe, we have George duh-byah's convention closer, a perfectly crafted Sept. 11th PR device designed to simultaneously play to the fears of Southern dolts and enrage tri-staters (and New Englanders) who were personally affected by the attacks.

Having watched the speech with a native New Yorker provided a line by line validation of my belief that Bush's continued milking of the 9/11 tragedy is nothing more than a disgraceful election tactic to rouse the NASCAR, 9/11 souveneir buying crowd. In Boston I think we are close enough to the tragedy to see when ethical lines are clearly crossed; when the issue has moved from healing wounds of a national tragedy to garnering single point percentage gains in key battleground states. But in having the convention in Manhattan, the Republicans have shown machiavellian acuity at the cost of sincerity. And it is sickening.

Bush's speech was merely a collection of reactionary rallying cries crafted to appeal to the inherent desire of all Americans to see their country safe and happy. The true Orwellian genius here is how Bush insists the natural human desire for safety is inexorably linked to the Republican sociopolitical platform. It is further designed to blur the line between the Iraq war and the war on terrorism, to blur the justification of that war from weapons of mass destruction to removing a despot: "Do I forget the lessons of September 11th and take the word of a madman, or do I take action to defend our country? Faced with that choice, I will defend America every time." Repeated statements by the 9/11 Comission that Iraq and al-Qaida were not cooperating in any way are brushed to the side.

The Kerry campaign needs to point these things out and furthermore it needs to express to the American people that the world is not black and white, right and wrong, good and evil. It is a complicated, highly nuanced world, and we need an intelligent, dare I say brilliant leader who can understand details and intricacies and generate sincere, well thought out strategies based on the aggregation and extrapolation of these data. Anyone with a brain and a computer can easily learn the basic governing philosophies of Hussein's Ba'athist movement and Osama bin Laden's al-Qaida.

The Ba'athist party emerged in the 1960s as (ironically) a pan-Arab socialist alternative to despotic right-wing monarchies. It was wholly secular (sometimes Atheist) and followed for some time the principles of Socialism. al-Qaida is rooted in the ultra-orthodox religious backlash to the Arab socialist movement. It seeks full integration of mosque and state and full implementation of Islamic Sharia law. It effectively advocates a return to the style of life of the 7th century A.D. Now bear in mind, Hussein's government hardly represented Socialism as it was intended, but it is clear to see that the two philosophies are squarely incompatible.

Bush's speech was light on the economy and bin Laden, light on future plans other than to say he wishes to see the power of corporations and HMOs protected, and the power of the people to seek a redress of grievances eliminated or suppressed. He made Afghanistan and Iraq seem like the Cirque de Soleil, having failed to mention that kidnapping and murder are rife in Baghdad and the Hamid Karzai government of Afghanistan only controls the capital of Kabul, the remainder of the country is in the hands of warlords, much as it was for the past twenty years.

My prediction of a second Bush term: increasing interest rates, lagging job growth, tax reform to favor multinational conglomorates, tort reform to favor corporations, a further blurring of the separation of church and state, and, dreadfully, perhaps Supreme Court appointments of right-wing reactionaries. A thought that should rightly shake anyone of reason to the bone.

RNC Week: Analysis Three

By
Nick DeLena
on September 3, 2004 2:47 PM | Permalink | Comments (4) | TrackBacks (0)

Zell Miller.

Admittedly, I was concerned when I heard about Zell Miller speaking at the Republican National Convention. It's always damaging when one of your own (debatable) breaks ranks and goes on national television to tell America why your party doesn't deserve the vote. I was concerned. But after I saw his speech, reminiscent of George Wallace and the hate-filled segregationists of the 1960s, I realized that it would probably do more damage to George Bush's reelection than to Kerry's campaign. Granted, Miller's style is inflamatory and sensationalistic, but this time his age shown and his speechwriter's lack of preparedness also shown; the main themes of his Diatribe Against Democrats (DAD) are wholly without substance.

His main points were: 1) Democrats view American troops as "occupiers" and therefore are treasonous, 2) Kerry does not support the U.S. military as demonstrated by his votes against certain military budgets that would have expanded production orders for the B-1, B-2, F-14, F-15, F-16, M-1 and Apache fighting vehicles, 3) something about nobody being like Wendell Wilkie anymore.

1) Interestingly, it was George Bush who first referred to American troops as occupiers.
April 13, 2004 8pm prime time press conference.
"As a proud and independent people, Iraqis do not support an indefinite occupation - and neither does America."
"They're not happy they're occupied. I wouldn't be happy if I were occupied either."

May 24, 2004, 8pm, press conference from the US Army War College, Carlisle, PA.
"The occupation will end, and Iraqis will govern their own affairs."

May 25, 2004, interview with Australian Broadcasting Corporation's John Shovelan.
"On June 30th, the Coalition Provisional Authority will cease to exist and will not be replaced. The occupation will end, and Iraqis will govern their own affairs."

I would be willing to bet that Bush has referred to our troops more as occupiers than Democrats have.

2) Kerry in his tenure in the Senate had approved 16 of 19 budgets. The ones he didn't approve, interestingly, Dick Cheney, as Secretary of Defense under George H. W. Bush also did not approve of. He was famous for imposing strict spending controls. Cheney at the time said: "You've squabbled and sometimes bickered and horse-traded and ended up forcing me to spend money on weapons that don't fill a vital need in these times of tight budgets and new requirements." Referring to the M-1, F-14 and F-16 as "great weapons" that we "have enough of."

3) Of Wendell Wilkie, Miller says: "In 1940, Wendell Wilkie was the Republican nominee. And there's no better example of someone repealing their 'private plans' than this good man. He gave Roosevelt the critical support he needed for a peacetime draft, an unpopular idea at the time. And he made it clear that he would rather lose the election than make national security a partisan campaign issue."

Actually, that's not quite accurate. Wilkie campaigned against Roosevelt's New Deal and advocated increased military spending. Roosevelt preempted that by expanding military contracts during the campaign - so Wilkie switched directions and accused the president of "warmongering." The election was held (Miller makes it sound like Wilkie withdrew) with Roosevelt garnering 449 electoral votes to Wilkie's 42. It was only after this election that Wilkie decided to support Roosevelt, eventually becoming his personal representative to Britain.

Zell Miller's style is light on facts and heavy on fiery southern-fried rhetoric.

I did some additional investigating into Miller's past and found something that sheds some light on his oratorical style (and perhaps his nascent party-shift). In the 1960s, Miller was the Chief of Staff for Lester Maddox, Georgia governor and segregationist. Maddox was so virulently racist that he distributed axe handles to patrons of his family-owned restaurant the night the 1964 Civil Rights Act was passed, to keep African Americans from entering his restaurant. That same night he chased protesters away with a pistol. As Governor he openly advocated defiance of the act. And now we're supposed to find Miller a credible critic of the Democratic party. Well frankly, our party is much better without him and his new allegiance to the GOP is just as fitting.

A concerted effort to root out poverty and political and economic injustice in the middle east might be a good start.
