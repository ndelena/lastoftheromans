RNC Week: Analysis Three

By
Nick DeLena
on September 3, 2004 2:47 PM | Permalink | Comments (4) | TrackBacks (0)

Zell Miller.

Admittedly, I was concerned when I heard about Zell Miller speaking at the Republican National Convention. It's always damaging when one of your own (debatable) breaks ranks and goes on national television to tell America why your party doesn't deserve the vote. I was concerned. But after I saw his speech, reminiscent of George Wallace and the hate-filled segregationists of the 1960s, I realized that it would probably do more damage to George Bush's reelection than to Kerry's campaign. Granted, Miller's style is inflamatory and sensationalistic, but this time his age shown and his speechwriter's lack of preparedness also shown; the main themes of his Diatribe Against Democrats (DAD) are wholly without substance.

His main points were: 1) Democrats view American troops as "occupiers" and therefore are treasonous, 2) Kerry does not support the U.S. military as demonstrated by his votes against certain military budgets that would have expanded production orders for the B-1, B-2, F-14, F-15, F-16, M-1 and Apache fighting vehicles, 3) something about nobody being like Wendell Wilkie anymore.

1) Interestingly, it was George Bush who first referred to American troops as occupiers.
April 13, 2004 8pm prime time press conference.
"As a proud and independent people, Iraqis do not support an indefinite occupation - and neither does America."
"They're not happy they're occupied. I wouldn't be happy if I were occupied either."

May 24, 2004, 8pm, press conference from the US Army War College, Carlisle, PA.
"The occupation will end, and Iraqis will govern their own affairs."

May 25, 2004, interview with Australian Broadcasting Corporation's John Shovelan.
"On June 30th, the Coalition Provisional Authority will cease to exist and will not be replaced. The occupation will end, and Iraqis will govern their own affairs."

I would be willing to bet that Bush has referred to our troops more as occupiers than Democrats have.

2) Kerry in his tenure in the Senate had approved 16 of 19 budgets. The ones he didn't approve, interestingly, Dick Cheney, as Secretary of Defense under George H. W. Bush also did not approve of. He was famous for imposing strict spending controls. Cheney at the time said: "You've squabbled and sometimes bickered and horse-traded and ended up forcing me to spend money on weapons that don't fill a vital need in these times of tight budgets and new requirements." Referring to the M-1, F-14 and F-16 as "great weapons" that we "have enough of."

3) Of Wendell Wilkie, Miller says: "In 1940, Wendell Wilkie was the Republican nominee. And there's no better example of someone repealing their 'private plans' than this good man. He gave Roosevelt the critical support he needed for a peacetime draft, an unpopular idea at the time. And he made it clear that he would rather lose the election than make national security a partisan campaign issue."

Actually, that's not quite accurate. Wilkie campaigned against Roosevelt's New Deal and advocated increased military spending. Roosevelt preempted that by expanding military contracts during the campaign - so Wilkie switched directions and accused the president of "warmongering." The election was held (Miller makes it sound like Wilkie withdrew) with Roosevelt garnering 449 electoral votes to Wilkie's 42. It was only after this election that Wilkie decided to support Roosevelt, eventually becoming his personal representative to Britain.

Zell Miller's style is light on facts and heavy on fiery southern-fried rhetoric.

I did some additional investigating into Miller's past and found something that sheds some light on his oratorical style (and perhaps his nascent party-shift). In the 1960s, Miller was the Chief of Staff for Lester Maddox, Georgia governor and segregationist. Maddox was so virulently racist that he distributed axe handles to patrons of his family-owned restaurant the night the 1964 Civil Rights Act was passed, to keep African Americans from entering his restaurant. That same night he chased protesters away with a pistol. As Governor he openly advocated defiance of the act. And now we're supposed to find Miller a credible critic of the Democratic party. Well frankly, our party is much better without him and his new allegiance to the GOP is just as fitting.
