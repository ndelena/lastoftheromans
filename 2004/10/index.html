A Red Sox Presidency

By
Nick DeLena
on October 28, 2004 3:32 PM | Permalink | Comments (22) | TrackBacks (0)

As a life-long Massachusetts boy, it's been hard for me to accept the Red Sox as anything other than perennial heartbreakers and September slumpers. Despite two very recent Patriots Super Bowl victories, and countless Celtics titles over the years (though not recently - 1986 again), the Red Sox are still Boston's favorite sons. And so the city that lives day to day knowing the Sox will fail in the end just had its mind blown. Everything we have known to be right and true was just washed away on a cold October night, 86 years to the day from the 1918 victory, in our last challenge since 1986, under a lunar eclipse. Boston is ascendant.

Even though I was hoping to play the Astros to have a little Massachusetts v. Texas, Kerry v. Bush parallel, we'll take beating another red state. After having won the superbowl, having hosted the Democratic National Convention, and having won the world series, Massachusetts is ascendant.

Next stop: The White House.

Bush: I will support undemocratic Iraq

By
Nick DeLena
on October 19, 2004 10:40 AM | Permalink | Comments (30) | TrackBacks (0)

In an interview with the AP, Bush yesterday said he would support and accept an Iraqi government run by Islamists, as long as there was a free and fair democratic process leading up to that election.

"I will be disappointed. But democracy is democracy," Bush said. Not surprisingly Bush draws no distinction between "democracy" and "theocracy." In an Arab world that is increasingly poorer, younger, and more radical, don't we run the risk of creating another Taliban-style Iraq? What exactly are the implications of an "Islamic Iraq" - Sharia law? The Islamists may come to power in the first and last free election in Iraq. We'd effectively be giving the keys away to al-Qaeda.

UPDATE: The latest polls in Iraq show religious leaders in the lead for the country's January election. From MSNBC: "Leaders of Iraq's religious parties have emerged as the country's most popular politicians and would win the largest share of votes if an election were held today, while the U.S.-backed government of interim Prime Minister Ayad Allawi is losing serious ground, according to a U.S.-financed poll by the International Republican Institute."

In another interesting note, 45% of Iraqis feel their country is heading in the wrong direction. That's actually less than the 56% of Americans that feel their country is heading in the wrong direction.

An even-handed attempt at reform?

By
Nick DeLena
on October 17, 2004 3:43 PM | Permalink | Comments (7) | TrackBacks (0)

In the Vice Presidential debate on October 5th, John Edwards, on the topic of tort reform and frivolous lawsuits introduced an interesting proposal to reduce the amount of lawsuits (thereby reducing liabilities and ideally premiums) that hit corporations across many sectors, notably health care.

"John Kerry and I have a plan to do something about it. We want to put more responsibility on the lawyers to require, before a case, malpractice, which the vice president just spoke about, have the case reviewed by independent experts to determine if the case is serious and meritorious before it can be filed; hold the lawyers responsible for that, certify that and hold the lawyer financially responsible if they don't do it; have a three-strikes-and-you're-out rule so that a lawyer who files three of these cases without meeting this requirement loses their right to file these cases.

That way we keep the cases out of the system that don't belong in the system. They talk about frivolous cases. We believe cases that don't belong in the system should never be in the system."

This is a bit of a follow up to an earlier post "The question of 'wealth" which took on interesting form in the comments regarding the relationship between health care costs and tort reform.

So far, Edwards's proposal is the most appealing I've heard. I agree that overweight people should not be able to sue McDonalds and that if you spill hot coffee on yourself, you are simply bumbling and clumsy, not an instant litigant. And I find it considerably suspect how multi-million dollar payouts magically heal "emotional damage." My concerns, however, arise from the potential restriction of a person's ability to seek a redress of grievances that some of the more pro-corporate reform proposals represent.

Proof of Italian stereotyping

By
Nick DeLena
on October 16, 2004 5:31 PM | Permalink | Comments (15) | TrackBacks (0)

Most of you that know me personally have heard me at least once talk about my hatred of the negative stereotyping of Italian-Americans in the media. Well thanks be to Allah that there are other people out there who also happen to be Italian and feel as outraged.

The Order Sons of Italy in America foundation and the National Italian American Foundation have both done extensive research of the effect of the portrayal of Italians as criminals or thugs.

A Zogby poll in early 2003 of American teenagers aged 13 to 18 revealed that 78% associate Italian Americans with either criminal activities or blue-collar work.

Another poll by Response Analysis Corp revealed that 74% of adult Americans believe most Italian Americans have some connection to organized crime.

The Sons of Italy's study of American media report the majority of advertisements featuring Italian Americans "use stereotypes that present an unbalanced portrayal of people of Italian heritage" noting that:

Italian men appear uneducated, dishonest and/or violent.

Women are elderly, overweight housewives and grandmothers wearing black dresses, housecoats or aprons.

U. S. Department of Justice estimates less than .0025% of the 26 million Italian-Americans are involved in organized crime.

The U.S. Census Bureau reports that two-thirds of the Italian Americans in the workforce are in white-collar jobs as executives, physicians, teachers, attorneys, administrators, etc.

Italian Americans are the fifth largest ethnic group in the United States.

UPDATE: Another topic I failed to touch upon is the prevailing mindset of a large element of Italian-Americans that aspires to live up to and perpetuate these stereotypes. Growing up in Revere, Mass, home of the hairy chest/gold-chain/pinky ring combination, I've seen it all my life. It's almost as if Italian-Americans, through the media's incessant berating, have become a parody of themselves, instead of proud inheritors of nearly three millenia of Italian and Roman art, literature, law, architecture, philosophy and culture. The perception of Italian-Americans can only improve if we stop propagating the stereotype. I blame this on dirty Sicilians who were never really Italian anyway. Sorry, Tom.

Bush hates Massachusetts

By
Nick DeLena
on October 15, 2004 3:55 PM | Permalink | Comments (12) | TrackBacks (0)

Is anyone else sick of hearing Bush use Massachusetts as an insult against Kerry? I don't understand how a state can be used as a wedge issue. Are we really so different up here that the mere mention of the word "Massachusetts" will cause ordinary Midwesterners and Southerners to shiver and vote Bush?

Why doesn't Kerry come out and stand up for the state that's sent him to Washington three consecutive times? We're doing it right! Texas is doing it wrong! Let the country know!

"When you're a senator from Massachusetts, when you're a colleague of Ted Kennedy..."

"As a matter of fact, your record is such that Ted Kennedy, your colleague, is the conservative senator from Massachusetts."

"Only a liberal senator from Massachusetts would say that a 49 percent increase in funding for education was not enough."

We are better than Texas in every quantifiable category because of our policies and our beliefs. I've written about this at length largely because I'm so frustrated at the increasing marginalization of Massachusetts (and New England in general) in American politics. Shouldn't leaders from the most successful regions be supported for their successful leadership? How did George Bush get elected on his record as Governor of Texas, a state which under his reign dropped in nearly every quantifiable category (education, environmental issues, disposable income, social spending) and had the most amount of state-sponsored executions than ever before during the reign of a single Governor. To quote Walter Sobchak, our bowling friend from The Big Lebowski, "Has the whole world gone crazy?"

Labour pays the price

By
Nick DeLena
on October 7, 2004 1:44 PM | Permalink | Comments (9) | TrackBacks (0)

There was a remarkable by-election in English city of Hartlepool last week. It is remarkable in two ways: 1) Tony Blair's Labour nearly lost one of their strongholds 2) The Tories (Conservatives) have been pushed to 4th place (!) behind the Liberal Democrats, and the UK Independence Party.

In the election graph at left, you can see the current election result compared with the percentage change from the last election. What this means to me is that voters are increasingly looking for an alternative to Blair's Labour, and are looking to alternative parties, not the Tories for that change.

In more international elections worth watching, the first test since the Iraq war for Conservative Australian PM John Howard comes this Saturday as he seeks to hold his job for a fourth term against Labour's Mark Latham.
