Proof of Italian stereotyping

By
Nick DeLena
on October 16, 2004 5:31 PM | Permalink | Comments (15) | TrackBacks (0)

Most of you that know me personally have heard me at least once talk about my hatred of the negative stereotyping of Italian-Americans in the media. Well thanks be to Allah that there are other people out there who also happen to be Italian and feel as outraged.

The Order Sons of Italy in America foundation and the National Italian American Foundation have both done extensive research of the effect of the portrayal of Italians as criminals or thugs.

A Zogby poll in early 2003 of American teenagers aged 13 to 18 revealed that 78% associate Italian Americans with either criminal activities or blue-collar work.

Another poll by Response Analysis Corp revealed that 74% of adult Americans believe most Italian Americans have some connection to organized crime.

The Sons of Italy's study of American media report the majority of advertisements featuring Italian Americans "use stereotypes that present an unbalanced portrayal of people of Italian heritage" noting that:

Italian men appear uneducated, dishonest and/or violent.

Women are elderly, overweight housewives and grandmothers wearing black dresses, housecoats or aprons.

U. S. Department of Justice estimates less than .0025% of the 26 million Italian-Americans are involved in organized crime.

The U.S. Census Bureau reports that two-thirds of the Italian Americans in the workforce are in white-collar jobs as executives, physicians, teachers, attorneys, administrators, etc.

Italian Americans are the fifth largest ethnic group in the United States.

UPDATE: Another topic I failed to touch upon is the prevailing mindset of a large element of Italian-Americans that aspires to live up to and perpetuate these stereotypes. Growing up in Revere, Mass, home of the hairy chest/gold-chain/pinky ring combination, I've seen it all my life. It's almost as if Italian-Americans, through the media's incessant berating, have become a parody of themselves, instead of proud inheritors of nearly three millenia of Italian and Roman art, literature, law, architecture, philosophy and culture. The perception of Italian-Americans can only improve if we stop propagating the stereotype. I blame this on dirty Sicilians who were never really Italian anyway. Sorry, Tom.

By Jimmy C. on October 18, 2004 12:56 PM

Interestingly, the HBO series The Sopranos, touted by Nick and his ilk (I just like that word, no negative implication intended) as being derogatory toward the cultural image of Italian-Americans, has actually had a few very interesting and worthy episodes dealing with this very subject - the mafiosi versus the more typical Italian-American families.

I agree there's certainly a problem when a vast majority of the country associates all Italian-Americans with organized crime. Conversely, I don't think that it's necessarily negative that the Italian Mafia has become a fixture of the American consciousness.

Italian-Americans are right in not wanting to be associated with this stereotype, but the unfortunate truth is that Italian organized crime exists, has existed, and likely will always exist. The mafia played a key role, for good or bad, in American history, particularly during and post-Prohibition (those who detest the mafia can blame the Puritan influences that led to the illegalizing of alcohol and the consequent and causal creation, or at least solidification, of the mafia). Clearly, for the most part, this is a darker side of our history we might rather put aside, but history it remains.

Moreover, this dark factor has undeniably led to some great art: aside from the obvious movie examples, the topic as a whole is representative of the darker side of this nation and the moral repugnancy of which we are all capable.

The nature of American history and society led inexorably to the creation of organized crime: the atmosphere in the early 20th Century almost necessitated the forming of such groups, groups who could take care of their people (for a price) in the absence of reasonable government. These origins are perhaps more noble, in this respect, than the Mob as it exists today, wherein they have become simple murdering thugs.

My point being, like it or not, the mafia is an undeniable and influential part of modern Italian-American, and most likely Italian, history.
