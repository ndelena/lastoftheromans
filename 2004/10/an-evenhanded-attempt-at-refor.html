An even-handed attempt at reform?

By
Nick DeLena
on October 17, 2004 3:43 PM | Permalink | Comments (7) | TrackBacks (0)

In the Vice Presidential debate on October 5th, John Edwards, on the topic of tort reform and frivolous lawsuits introduced an interesting proposal to reduce the amount of lawsuits (thereby reducing liabilities and ideally premiums) that hit corporations across many sectors, notably health care.

"John Kerry and I have a plan to do something about it. We want to put more responsibility on the lawyers to require, before a case, malpractice, which the vice president just spoke about, have the case reviewed by independent experts to determine if the case is serious and meritorious before it can be filed; hold the lawyers responsible for that, certify that and hold the lawyer financially responsible if they don't do it; have a three-strikes-and-you're-out rule so that a lawyer who files three of these cases without meeting this requirement loses their right to file these cases.

That way we keep the cases out of the system that don't belong in the system. They talk about frivolous cases. We believe cases that don't belong in the system should never be in the system."

This is a bit of a follow up to an earlier post "The question of 'wealth" which took on interesting form in the comments regarding the relationship between health care costs and tort reform.

So far, Edwards's proposal is the most appealing I've heard. I agree that overweight people should not be able to sue McDonalds and that if you spill hot coffee on yourself, you are simply bumbling and clumsy, not an instant litigant. And I find it considerably suspect how multi-million dollar payouts magically heal "emotional damage." My concerns, however, arise from the potential restriction of a person's ability to seek a redress of grievances that some of the more pro-corporate reform proposals represent.

By Jimmy C. on October 20, 2004 5:26 PM

Policy-wise, I would tend to agree with you. I'm just trying to look at it how a court might.

It depends, ultimately, I suppose, on the precise language of the manual itself. If it said "Cruise control, when activated, will automatically maintain a set speed without depression of the gas pedal," then it's pretty clear that it certainly DOES NOT control steering as well.

But what if the language is more vague? If it says "Cruise control functions automatically to maintain operation of the vehicle" or something equally ambiguous, it wouldn't be too hard to argue that it could not be reasonably inferred that steering was not included in this function.

I'm beating a dead horse here, I realize. The best answer is that a reasonable person probably would know that a car cannot drive itself. Even in the case of an idiosyncratic plaintiff (one who had never driven before), you'd need to prove knowledge of this idiosyncracy on the part of the defendants.

The point is that lawsuits that make it to trial are from a legal standpoint never "stupid," in the absence of a serious flaw in the legal system. Cases at trial have already established a viable legal basis for argument, or they would not have gotten that far.

Two things.

One, I'm very suspicious of these proposed "independent experts." I suppose what they mean, from a legal perspective, is that other legal experts would examine cases to see whether a viable cause of action exists...As DA said, it's very unlikely that these "experts" could accurately predict the viability of a case in the discovery or pre-trial phases.

Moreover, every citizen has a right to his day in court. This is what courts do: separate the wheat from the chaff, the winners from the losers. As long as a party has a viable cause of action and some sufficient evidence to back up that cause of action, a court must hear the case. Keep in mind that true frivolous lawsuits rarely if ever reach trial, as they will typically be resolved via settlement (97% of cases are settled out of court). Additionally, any first year law student is capable of recognizing when a case does not have a real cause of action: cases like these are resolved in motions to dismiss.

Two, the American lega
l system takes "emotional damages," as you term them, very seriously. There is an entire section of tort law dedicated to the intentional infliction of emotional distress. When a party successfully proves that they have suffered emotional distress, the courts compensate them in the only way they can: monetarily. A court cannot undo the emotional harm done to a person, but it can see that that person is compensated financially for their suffering.

Remember that what may seem silly on the surface often has far more fundamental legal aspects to it than its appearance makes obvious.

A person getting severely burned from spilt coffee might seem like simply a klutz, but in legal terms, one could argue (and this is a quick and dirty version) that a reasonable person (the crux of many legal arguments) would certainly not expect McDonald's coffee to be hot enough to cause serious injury and that McDonald's was negligent in heating it to such an extent.

Note also that legal representation is extraordinarily expensive, and any case that gets to trial has already cost the parties thousands upon thousands of dollars, so your average person is going to be very reluctant and in fact incapable of litigating "frivolous" claims.

If a case makes it to trial, it is not frivolous, by legal standpoints. As for lawsuits that don't, any skilled and honest lawyer (flame away), though they be at the mercy of their client, will do their best to see that the case is settled as efficiently as possible.
