Supreme Court wakes up

By
Nick DeLena
on June 30, 2004 11:21 AM | Permalink | Comments (2) | TrackBacks (0)

It looks like the Supreme Court might have woken up and realized in order to preserve the checks and balances of the U.S. government, all three branches of the government must be active. In Hamdi v. Rumsfeld, decided June 28, 2004:

JUSTICE OíCONNOR, joined by THE CHIEF JUSTICE, JUSTICE KENNEDY, and JUSTICE BREYER, concluded that although Congress authorized the detention of combatants in the narrow circumstances alleged in this case, due process demands that a citizen held in the United States as an enemy combatant be given a meaningful opportunity to contest the factual basis for that detention before a neutral decisionmaker. Pp. 14ñ15. JUSTICE SOUTER, joined by JUSTICE GINSBURG, concluded that Hamdiís detention is unauthorized, but joined with the plurality to conclude that on remand Hamdi should have a meaningful opportunity to offer evidence that he is not an enemy combatant.

No surprise that Justice Scalia filed a dissenting opinion (which was probably written by Dick Cheney). From his opinion, it wasn't altogether clear what he was advocating...

¿Te gusta conducír?

By
Nick DeLena
on June 27, 2004 4:07 PM | Permalink | Comments (3) | TrackBacks (0)

As many of you know, I purchased a 1994 Volkswagen Jetta back in March. Now the car was a salvage; it had previously been in an accident and deemed a "total loss." I rebuilt the car and am now attempting to get it registered. But the Massachusetts bureacratic process apparently has other plans in mind. The first step is to get the car inspected by the state police. The car has twice failed the process: once because I drove the car there (you can't do this, but they don't tell you beforehand), and just today the car failed because airbag light never came on. An important safety feature of the car, no doubt, but this is not the Massachusetts safety inspection, it's a fraud/theft inspection to verify that you have indeed rebuilt the car with legitimate parts. The safety inspection is a separate process.

Either way, the car failed again and now I have to get the airbag fixed and certified by a VW dealer, so as we speak it's sitting at Boston Volkswagen in Allston. Remind me never to deal with salvage titles again.

Big Lies: Big Questionmark

By
Nick DeLena
on June 19, 2004 10:11 PM | Permalink | Comments (2) | TrackBacks (0)

Has anyone heard of Joe Conason? I leafed through his "Big Lies: The Right-Wing Propaganda Machine and How It Distorts the Truth" while in Borders downtown last week. Notably one of the first pages I thumbed across mentioned Noam Chomsky. Understandably, I thought he would be mentioned positively in a book of this type, but I was soon corrected:

Like so much other rightist cant, "liberals hate America" is a slogan designed to confuse and inflame the ignorant. And like many another successful frame-up, this one grossly exaggerates a small fact. On the far left there does exist a handful of annoying academics and activists -- typified by Noam Chomsky and Ramsey Clark -- whose ideas about America and the world haven't changed much since the '70s. Their politics hark back to a period when the criminal excesses of the Cold War in Indochina, Latin America and southern Africa had alienated many young Americans from our country. The overwhelming majority of those young people never contemplated any kind of unpatriotic act, and those who remained active in politics took up the challenge of democratic reform.

I'm not sure what the point of that paragraph could be other than to say that a leftist, Noam Chomsky, might well hate America. Isn't the first rule of opinion writing not to contradict your argument while making your argument? Wouldn't saying that 'leftists don't hate America but this leftist might' reduce your overall argumentative potency? Frankly I find the paragraph baffling and useless, and I say that not just because I am a fan of Chomsky; what point does it serve to criticise one of the left's most prolific activists if you yourself are writing a book to serve the leftist cause? And does he mean small faction? The Noam Chomsky and Ramsey Clark America-haters club? To infer that either of these esteemed intellectuals can "hate" America in such a non-specific and unreasoned manner serves to delegitimise what they fight for (the leftist cause). Obviously this guy isn't too serious.

Amazon.com's editorial review actually mentions Chomsky: "And he navigates a highly readable and informative writing style that feels more substantive than Molly Ivins and Al Franken but still a lot wittier than Noam Chomsky."

By "wittier" they must mean "pettier."

Allegedly, the book makes a factual argument that Republicans are not the "fiscal geniuses and champions of free enterprise" they pretend to be and that "the right's self-proclaimed monopoly on family values" is imaginary.

Also tackled by Conason: the conservative smearing of liberals as unpatriotic and anti-American, "And of course, the "compassionate conservatism" of George W. Bush."

I didn't buy the book after I read the line about Chomsky and Clark. But at least the premise is sound: factually proving that Republicans are the liars and hypocrites we passionately believe them to be.

"Change" leaders condemn Bush policy

By
Nick DeLena
on June 13, 2004 4:58 PM | Permalink | Comments (0) | TrackBacks (0)

A new document being released by a group called "Diplomats and Military Commanders for Change" condems Bush's foreign policy. The group is comprised of former Ambassadors and State Department officials and also retired military brass.

An unprecedented bipartisan coalition of 26 career chiefs of mission and retired four-star military leaders will launch a nationwide campaign to press for the need for change in U.S. foreign and defense policy because they are deeply concerned by the damage the Bush Administration has caused to our national and international interests.

Many of the officials were appointed by Republican administrations, including Phyllis Oakley, the deputy State Department spokeswoman during former President Ronald Reagan's second term and William Harrop, who was appointed ambassador to Israel by former President George Bush in 1991.

Dead Reagans sell no arms

By
Nick DeLena
on June 11, 2004 2:36 PM | Permalink | Comments (2) | TrackBacks (0)

I'm really getting sick of this nationwide media ejaculation over Reagan's death. Even some half-respectable organizations like CNN are dragging it out unnecesarily. Ok, he's dead. He really wasn't a great person, let's get over it.

Lish puts it best:

i'm glad the fucker is dead, why the fuck do i need to have compassion? because he was a old senile bastard and human? kiss my ass. i won't cry for him. i won't participate in the deification of bad person.

you want to rejoice reagan's legacy? join the fucking republican party. hold a non-stop bedside vigil for margaret thatcher. go burn a peasant village in central or south america. slash the budgets of federal programs which help poor women and children. close a union factory. force a woman to have a baby she doesn't want to have. deny HIV and AIDS exists. force gay people (especially men) back into the closet. etc etc etc. but don't expect me to pretend that i'm supposed to be some merciful & compassionate saint when it comes to his death.

I was reading comments on the BBC website regarding his death, about fifty Americans posted, claiming it was Reagan that ended the Cold War, they would have us believe it was single-handedly. There may be some truth to the fact that he helped expedite an inevitable process, but when I hear things like this I picture these lonely people getting inspiration from an imagined visage of Reagan riding horseback across Eastern Europe tearing down regime after regime finally making his way through Red Square and into the Kre
mlin where Gorbachev succumbs instantly to the sheer force of Reagan's capitalist will. Ugh. Honestly.

He drove the country into a massive defense spending frenzy, earning many deficit trophies, peerless indeed until Bush the lesser came along. And then you can count among his atrocities Nicaragua, Grenada, Libya, Afghanistan, El Salvador, Lebanon, Honduras, the selling of chemical weapons to Saddam Hussein in 1984, the destruction of an Iranian passenger jet in 1987,etc.etc. an impressive list for a pre-neocon. And now he's dead. So what.

It's all just a seattle/washington/prague 00/68/chicago/nixon/reagan circle-fighting machine.

wie Regen auf einem Grab

By
Nick DeLena
on June 5, 2004 3:25 PM | Permalink | Comments (5) | TrackBacks (0)

Neglectful I have been, most neglectful. In starting this entry off, I'm reminded of the countless blogs you see across the net where every entry decries the lack of more frequent updates, leaving the site generally bereft of content. I don't want this to turn into one of those sites.

I'm back on Beacon Hill. I know, I'm all over the place. I've decided to move back until the lease expires. I never found a subletter, so I'm reoccupying once-native territory. Like Belisarius retaking Rome in 536: "The Rome that Belisarius entered reflected the general decline of the western empire. Though still the largest city in the west, its population had shrunk, people drove cattle through the forums, and buildings destroyed by the Visigoths and Vandals in the last century had not been repaired."

I reentered the apartment to find furniture unkempt and broken, cupboards raided and vacant, my room empty and dust-filled, and my roommate half-starved and xenophobic. But I come to integrate, not to conquer, just as Belisarius came at the behest of Justinian to rid the west of barbarians. An ambitious and ultimately unsuccessful expedition. Wie Regen auf einem Grab - "like rain on a grave."

I've been wholly occupied with coursework & web design of late.
