Big Lies: Big Questionmark

By
Nick DeLena
on June 19, 2004 10:11 PM | Permalink | Comments (2) | TrackBacks (0)

Has anyone heard of Joe Conason? I leafed through his "Big Lies: The Right-Wing Propaganda Machine and How It Distorts the Truth" while in Borders downtown last week. Notably one of the first pages I thumbed across mentioned Noam Chomsky. Understandably, I thought he would be mentioned positively in a book of this type, but I was soon corrected:

Like so much other rightist cant, "liberals hate America" is a slogan designed to confuse and inflame the ignorant. And like many another successful frame-up, this one grossly exaggerates a small fact. On the far left there does exist a handful of annoying academics and activists -- typified by Noam Chomsky and Ramsey Clark -- whose ideas about America and the world haven't changed much since the '70s. Their politics hark back to a period when the criminal excesses of the Cold War in Indochina, Latin America and southern Africa had alienated many young Americans from our country. The overwhelming majority of those young people never contemplated any kind of unpatriotic act, and those who remained active in politics took up the challenge of democratic reform.

I'm not sure what the point of that paragraph could be other than to say that a leftist, Noam Chomsky, might well hate America. Isn't the first rule of opinion writing not to contradict your argument while making your argument? Wouldn't saying that 'leftists don't hate America but this leftist might' reduce your overall argumentative potency? Frankly I find the paragraph baffling and useless, and I say that not just because I am a fan of Chomsky; what point does it serve to criticise one of the left's most prolific activists if you yourself are writing a book to serve the leftist cause? And does he mean small faction? The Noam Chomsky and Ramsey Clark America-haters club? To infer that either of these esteemed intellectuals can "hate" America in such a non-specific and unreasoned manner serves to delegitimise what they fight for (the leftist cause). Obviously this guy isn't too serious.

Amazon.com's editorial review actually mentions Chomsky: "And he navigates a highly readable and informative writing style that feels more substantive than Molly Ivins and Al Franken but still a lot wittier than Noam Chomsky."

By "wittier" they must mean "pettier."

Allegedly, the book makes a factual argument that Republicans are not the "fiscal geniuses and champions of free enterprise" they pretend to be and that "the right's self-proclaimed monopoly on family values" is imaginary.

Also tackled by Conason: the conservative smearing of liberals as unpatriotic and anti-American, "And of course, the "compassionate conservatism" of George W. Bush."

I didn't buy the book after I read the line about Chomsky and Clark. But at least the premise is sound: factually proving that Republicans are the liars and hypocrites we passionately believe them to be.
