The Falluja killing

By
Nick DeLena
on November 17, 2004 4:39 PM | Permalink | Comments (21) | TrackBacks (0)

Doubtless most of you have heard of the recent incident in a Falluja mosque, where a U.S. marine allegedly shot a wounded prisoner in the head at point-blank range. It was caught on video and aired (partially) on major U.S. networks. Obviously this is illegal (even if the insurgents are not franc-tireur), and the soldier at the center of this has been removed from duty and may face charges. The biggest shame in all of this, however, is not the killing of the prisoner, or the possible trial and imprisonment of the marine, it's that any of this had to happen in the first place - that young men barely twenty have been denied their youth and have been forced into this sickening world of fear and death and have their lives be under constant threat. It is no wonder they are making mistakes and are standoffish with Iraqis, they're probably sick to death from fear. The rules of warfare have been thrown away. It is hard to blame the marine's action; a day before the incident, one of his comrades was killed by explosives stuffed in a dead insurgent's body - the type of attacks American troops are facing daily across Iraq. "Everybody who looks daily into the cold eye of death and gazes on so many dead faces bearing the stamp of suffering and renunciation, becomes certainly callous, but also old, very very old."

Can we say with full conviction that this is a justified war?
