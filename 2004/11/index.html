The Falluja killing

By
Nick DeLena
on November 17, 2004 4:39 PM | Permalink | Comments (21) | TrackBacks (0)

Doubtless most of you have heard of the recent incident in a Falluja mosque, where a U.S. marine allegedly shot a wounded prisoner in the head at point-blank range. It was caught on video and aired (partially) on major U.S. networks. Obviously this is illegal (even if the insurgents are not franc-tireur), and the soldier at the center of this has been removed from duty and may face charges. The biggest shame in all of this, however, is not the killing of the prisoner, or the possible trial and imprisonment of the marine, it's that any of this had to happen in the first place - that young men barely twenty have been denied their youth and have been forced into this sickening world of fear and death and have their lives be under constant threat. It is no wonder they are making mistakes and are standoffish with Iraqis, they're probably sick to death from fear. The rules of warfare have been thrown away. It is hard to blame the marine's action; a day before the incident, one of his comrades was killed by explosives stuffed in a dead insurgent's body - the type of attacks American troops are facing daily across Iraq. "Everybody who looks daily into the cold eye of death and gazes on so many dead faces bearing the stamp of suffering and renunciation, becomes certainly callous, but also old, very very old."

Can we say with full conviction that this is a justified war?

Canada's embrace of progressives

By
Nick DeLena
on November 15, 2004 11:51 AM | Permalink | Comments (35) | TrackBacks (0)

Since the disasterous embarassing reactionary U.S. presidential election, several sites have sprung up providing information on applying for Canadian residency. The latest website is Canadian Alternative.

The site says: "We invite you to get to know Canada," the site says. "Explore the richness and diversity of our regions. And find out why Canada is the perfect alternative for conscientious, forward-thinking Americans."

Reasons to move to Canada, as cited by www.canadianalternative.com:

1. Canada has universal public health care.

2. Canada has no troops in Iraq.

3. Canada signed the Kyoto Protocol environmental treaty.

4. More than half of Canada's provinces allow same-sex marriage.

5. The Canadian Senate recommends legalizing marijuana.

6. Canada has no law restricting abortion.

7. Canada has strict gun laws and relatively little violence.

8. The United Nations has ranked Canada the best country to live in for eight consecutive years.

9. Canada abolished the death penalty in 1976.

10. Canada has not run a federal deficit since 1996-97.


"Progressive, prosperous, inclusive: feel at home in Canada."
I really believe that Canada is becoming more American than America.

Another

By
Nick DeLena
on November 12, 2004 5:07 PM | Permalink | Comments (4) | TrackBacks (0)

Much of southern New England is currently getting the season's first real taste of snow with accumulations of up to five inches expected before the storm moves out of the region on Saturday. The National Weather Service at Taunton in an advisory issued shortly after noon said the heaviest snowfall would come after midnight into Saturday morning. The areas expected to be hardest hit are interior southern Rhode Island and south coastal Massachusetts which includes New Bedford and Fall River. The forecast calls for between three and five inches of snow in those areas. The early stages of the storm has seen a mixture of snow and rain across most of southern New England which also includes northern Rhode Island and northern Connecticut. The NWS advisory said temperatures would drop steadily during the night with lows ranging from 30 to 35 in the Boston area while well inland the readings would be between 25 and 30 degrees.

I took the above picture about a week ago, through my living room window. Currently - just one week later - the tree is nearly leafless, covered in wet slush, what leaves are left are frozen and shriveled. Welcome to another New England winter.




Red America Blues

By
Nick DeLena
on November 7, 2004 3:53 PM | Permalink | Comments (25) | TrackBacks (0)

On CNN, David Gergen, a former top-level aide to both Ronald Reagan and Bill Clinton, approached this cultural divide from an entirely different perspective: from that of blue-state liberals who canít believe what happened this week. "Thereís going to be a sense of alienation, a lot of isolation from the majority," Gergen said. He even went so far as to observe that many liberals are going to find themselves wondering "what kind of a country" theyíre living in.

Nowhere is that sense of isolation, of alienation, going to be felt more keenly than in Massachusetts, Kerryís home and the bluest of blue states. We live, after all, in the only state where same-sex marriage is legal, protected by the state constitution. It is a state that both Bushes bitterly mocked in their successful presidential campaigns, with George W. sneering that Kerry was a "Massachusetts liberal." Itís possible, of course, to make too much of this: 37 percent of Massachusetts voters cast their ballots against Kerry on Tuesday, just as 34 percent of Texas voters opposed Bush. There are plenty of red voters in blue states and blue in red. But for Massachusettsís urban, educated class ó the folks who work in medicine, higher education, or technology, live in or near the city, and value diversity ó what happened on Election Day was a terrible blow not just to their hopes for a Kerry victory, but to their very idea of what it means to be an American.

-Dan Kennedy

A shift to the left (to the south)

By
Nick DeLena
on November 1, 2004 9:53 PM | Permalink | Comments (0) | TrackBacks (0)

In yet another shift to the left in global politics, the South American country of Uruguay has elected Tabare Vazquez, head of the Broad Front-Progressive Encounter, a coalition of socialists, communists and social democrats what will be Uruguay's first leftist government in 170 years. Uruguay is the latest Latin American country to swing to the left, following Brazil's election of Luiz Inacio Lula da Silva (commonly known as "Lula'). The majority of Latin American countries are now leftist or center-left. His campaign was largely run on promises to challenge free trade policies that have been impoverishing native farmers.

Brazil, Chile, Venezuela and Argentina are now governed by parties of the left.
