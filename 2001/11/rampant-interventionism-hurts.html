Rampant interventionism hurts the United States

By
Nick DeLena
on November 25, 2001 11:03 PM | Permalink | Comments (0) | TrackBacks (0)

Americans have not become wise to the appallingly obvious truth that interventionism is directly responsible for the attacks of September 11. The mujahadeen guerillas started as an Islamist rebel movement, operating against the socialist government that was in place at the time. This was before the Soviets even invaded. The United States armed and trained the rank and file of the mujahadeen guerillas. After twenty-two years of meddling in the Middle Eas
t, the United States has earned the animosity of a great many peoples. We seem somehow surprised that the same people we worked with in the late 70s and 80s were affiliated with the radical Islamists who attacked us on September 11.

The United States may be the worldís only ìsuperpowerî, but surely that does not give it a mandate to ìpoliceî the globe in some kind of hunt for nobility or self-fulfillment. Violating the sovereignty of nations in acts of zealous altruistic military heroism is a farce; weíre assisting groups who dislike us against groups who dislike us even more. Intervening merely exacerbates anti-American sentiment.

In Chikeluba Kenechukwuís article in the November 14th print of the Suffolk Journal, entitled ìU.S. needs an interventionist foreign policyî, Kenechukwu states that the world benefits from American ìpolicingî. I feel this cannot be farther from the truth.

While I do not consider myself an isolationist, I do not support the type of unilateral military action Kenechukwu proposes America undertake. Kenechukwu states the world ìneeds a strong U.S.î What the world needs is a strong United Nations; a united front that achieves results through unanimity rather than unilateralism. While the United Nations is far from perfect, it is the only institution that derives its existence from the consensus of nations. The only consensus the President of the United States takes is that of his advisors (and not all of them at that). Should a group of ten or so people decide the fate of the world?

Kenechukwuís allusion that the United Statesí current anti-terror coalition-building efforts are part of some greater benevolence is naÔve. Do not for one instant believe anything the United States is doing right now is for the good of other nations. While removing al-Qaeda and the Taliban in Afghanistan is a necessary response to September 11th, it is merely a knee-jerk, posthumous reaction that will primarily benefit America. Will we also remove the root causes of this radicalism? Are Americans even aware of why these attacks occurred? The ideological media has portrayed the radicals as anti-democratic, anti-Christian, and standing against the very principles that America was founded upon. While this may be true with the Taliban, it is not true with the majority of middle easterners, many of whom hold a closet-admiration of Osama Bin Laden and his cause. These ordinary people do not hate America because it is democratic, or secular, they hate it because the United States supports repressive regimes in their homeland. We rob them of democracy, when we pretend we are bent on propagating it. The Cold War brings countless examples. The Saudi regime is perhaps the most applicable. There is no such thing as a bill of rights in Saudi Arabia. There is no freedom at all. Dissent garners you an execution. The United States formed a partnership with the Saudi monarchy in the mid-50s to act as a counterbalance to increasing Soviet influence in the Arab world. The United States has a dubious track record of turning a blind eye to the basic rights of people so as to not upset their oppressors. It is not coincidental that the United States has a considerable interest in Saudi oil. Do you think we will fight for the rights of the Saudi populous, with the risk of losing oil? We have not yet, and I strongly doubt we will. In Saudi Arabia, American support for this regime is blatant and quite obvious to the local population. They know we back their oppression, and they resent us for it. Is it any wonder, then, that their destitute poverty, lack of freedom, and lack of opportunity breeds their radicalism? To them, it may be their only hope of breaking the oppression.

Examples of American inconsistencies in its conduct of ìselect interventionismî are innumerable. The situation in Kosovo before the NATO-led military campaign can be compared to the suppression of the Kurds in southeastern Turkey. While we quickly aided the ethnic Albanian Kosovars against their Serb oppressors, we looked the other way when Turkey, one of our closest allies, conducted the systematic abolition of certain nationalist elements of Kurdish society. This has been the case since 1925.

Doug Bandow, a Senior Fellow at the Cato Institute, a national think-tank, testified before congress regarding NATOís intervention in Kosovo on March 10, 1999. Bandow asked ìWhat is the standard for making war?î He continued: ìYugoslavia has done nothing against America or any of its allies. Granted that Serbian treatment of Kosovars has been atrocious. So has the Turkish handling of Kurds. And the conduct of Indonesia in East Timor. As well as the behavior of two score other governments in a variety of conflicts around the globe. Is war the right remedy in these cases?î

Bandow also mentions Burundi, Cambodia, Rwanda, Sudan, Uganda, Angola, Congo, Ethiopia, Liberia, Mozambique, Sierra Leone, Sri Lanka, Armenia, Azerbaijan, Ethiopia, Eritrea, India, Pakistan, Mexico, Northern Ireland, Russia, Spain, Turkey, Burma, Georgia, India, Indonesia, and Tajikistan as an extensive list of countries or regions of active or potential geopolitical instability. If America does what Kenechukwu proposes, we are certainly in for a third world war. And for what end? Why should America send its sons and daughters to die in these places? These are the worldís problems, not Americaís alone. Certainly comprehensive, diplomatic solutions can be reached in the halls of the United Nations.

Conveniently, another example of American neglect for human rights brought up by Kenechukwu, is that of Nigeria. For the past 41 years, Nigeria has largely been run by various military dictatorships. The various regimes that have ruled Nigeria were supported by the United States as bulwarks to Soviet influence in the region. They were, with little or no exception, brutal. Thousands were executed, including, in 1995, prominent ethnic Ogoni environmentalist, dissident, and writer Ken Saro-Wiwa, who fought against the environmental degradation of the Niger River Delta. The river is one of the most petroleum-rich regions in the world. Shell Oil operates a large facility in that area, and has been helping, with the blessings of the dictatorships, and indirectly the United States, to repress its workers, its neighbors, and pollute the Delta.

While it is true that Nigeria returned to a civilian-based government in 1999 (which it does every seven years or so), does that mean that offers of support and collusion from President Olusegun Obasanjo should be accepted with open arms? What has he done for the protection of the Ogoni workers or the Niger River Delta?

The answer is irrelevant. The United States turns a blind-eye to abuses in exchange for absolute support. What gives a nation that conducts this kind of activity a mandate to decide what nations violate its concepts of international law? The Soviets invaded Hungary in 1956 in what they considered a noble intervention; to stop the pro-democracy rebellion from seizing government. To the Soviets it was a just cause. To us it was oppression. An individual nation cannot objectively act in the best interests of others.

The only just intervention is humanitarian intervention, which must be carried out by a united world; with the consensus of the majority. The only vehicle in place to do that today is the United Nations.
