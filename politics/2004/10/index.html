A Red Sox Presidency

By
Nick DeLena
on October 28, 2004 3:32 PM | Permalink | Comments (22) | TrackBacks (0)

As a life-long Massachusetts boy, it's been hard for me to accept the Red Sox as anything other than perennial heartbreakers and September slumpers. Despite two very recent Patriots Super Bowl victories, and countless Celtics titles over the years (though not recently - 1986 again), the Red Sox are still Boston's favorite sons. And so the city that lives day to day knowing the Sox will fail in the end just had its mind blown. Everything we have known to be right and true was just washed away on a cold October night, 86 years to the day from the 1918 victory, in our last challenge since 1986, under a lunar eclipse. Boston is ascendant.

Even though I was hoping to play the Astros to have a little Massachusetts v. Texas, Kerry v. Bush parallel, we'll take beating another red state. After having won the superbowl, having hosted the Democratic National Convention, and having won the world series, Massachusetts is ascendant.

Next stop: The White House.

Why not us?

Bush hates Massachusetts

By
Nick DeLena
on October 15, 2004 3:55 PM | Permalink | Comments (12) | TrackBacks (0)

Is anyone else sick of hearing Bush use Massachusetts as an insult against Kerry? I don't understand how a state can be used as a wedge issue. Are we really so different up here that the mere mention of the word "Massachusetts" will cause ordinary Midwesterners and Southerners to shiver and vote Bush?

Why doesn't Kerry come out and stand up for the state that's sent him to Washington three consecutive times? We're doing it right! Texas is doing it wrong! Let the country know!

"When you're a senator from Massachusetts, when you're a colleague of Ted Kennedy..."

"As a matter of fact, your record is such that Ted Kennedy, your colleague, is the conservative senator from Massachusetts."

"Only a liberal senator from Massachusetts would say that a 49 percent increase in funding for education was not enough."

We are better than Texas in every quantifiable category because of our policies and our beliefs. I've written about this at length largely because I'm so frustrated at the increasing marginalization of Massachusetts (and New England in general) in American politics. Shouldn't leaders from the most successful regions be supported for their successful leadership? How did George Bush get elected on his record as Governor of Texas, a state which under his reign dropped in nearly every quantifiable category (education, environmental issues, disposable income, social spending) and had the most amount of state-sponsored executions than ever before during the reign of a single Governor. To quote Walter Sobchak, our bowling friend from The Big Lebowski, "Has the whole world gone crazy?"
