The confuse & distract paradigm

By
Nick DeLena
on May 8, 2006 9:26 AM | Permalink | Comments (0) | TrackBacks (0)

Some of the work I do for a living is information technology audits as part of the Sarbanes-Oxley Act. On a high level this involves assembling a picture of a publically-traded company's IT environment and then matching this up against a set of standards and best practices. We request documentation and evidence and perform testing. Some of our client engagements are on behalf of a company's internal audit department to get them ready for "attestation" by an accounting firm, and some of our engagements are coming in externally to do attestation work. A repeating pattern emerges from those managers or executives whose business units have little or no functioning internal controls, what I like to call the "Confuse & Distract strategy." In short, when you request something, say a screen-print of their company-wide group password settings, they provide you with 400 pages of company policies, print-offs and general nonsense. When normally it would take you a half hour to examine evidence provided as requested, it now takes you much longer to make sense of what you've been given.

Enter Air Force General Michael Hayden. The latest player in President Bush's Confuse & Distract Vaudeville act on true intelligence reform. While the circumstances surrounding outgoing CIA Director Porter-Goss's departure are questionable at best, he was at least a civilian. A lot of legislators, from both sides of the aisle, are finding the nomination of Gen. Hayden troublesome in light of the warantless wiretapping debackle. Nominating military brass, not exactly known for being forthcoming to this top intelligence post is nothing more than a distraction, meant to delay, diffuse, and obfuscate the push for intelligence reform. Why choose this path? Bush, with approval ratings ebbing to near-Nixon-during-Watergate levels certainly needs his Republican colleagues' support at this time, yet this decision leaves most of them confounded. Nominating a military general for this position seems a rather defensive tactic by the president; building walls in desperate times, insulating key agencies and key information. It represents another example of this president governing in politics not policy.
