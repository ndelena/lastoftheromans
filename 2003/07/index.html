Edward Gibbon: You Are The Man.

By
Nick DeLena
on July 30, 2003 12:39 PM | Permalink | Comments (3)

I'm still ploughing through History of the Decline and Fall of the Roman Empire. This man (Edward Gibbon) amazes me. He wrote a seven volume set. Some 3,800 pages over 10 years detailing every significant development of the Roman Empire from 180A.D. to 1453 (the fall of Constantinople). The language is beautiful to boot.

This past weekend I went down to Barnstable on the Cape for a clambake. I think that's what they call it anyway. They had this big fire and they put seaweed over it and then placed the food on top of that to cook. Apparently this is commonly known as the clambake cooking method. You'd figure that myself, being from Massachusetts, should know this. I did not know this. I guess I'm too much of a city dweller.

Surely the best part of the afternoon was when I was accosted by a portly girl for wearing 'pleated' shorts. I really can't recall our conversation (read: heated argument) that well, but she basically accosted me. I don't think I spoke to her at all before she proverbially sank my Arizona. It was rather strange. In the course of her criticism of my shorts, it somehow came to be revealed that she is a conservative Republican. I'm not sure what or how this related to my shorts, but it came to pass. (Perhaps she overheard me talking politics?) I don't know.

inheritance of golden age values

By
Nick DeLena
on July 1, 2003 6:32 PM | Permalink | Comments (0) | TrackBacks (0)

did you ever feel like you were really on the cusp of something new?

i really thought i was for a while.

-

i had the best run of my life yesterday. around seven i left the apartment, ran down over the footbridge, onto the esplanade down to the mass ave bridge, over it, back along memorial drive in cambridge and back over the longfellow. 4.1 miles of high speed bipedal locomotion.

today i rode my trek all the way to newton and back.

sometimes i still feel like i don't exist on the same plane as a lot of people. like i can pass right by them and neither of us are sure that the other really exists.

yesterday i retrieved my film from cambridge st. photo. there is only one man who works in there and he reminds me of my grandfather that passed away three years ago. my grandfather was a photography expert, though not through any peaceful means. he was a member of naval reconnaisance during the korean war. in those days that meant leaning out the side of a helicopter with a 16mm camera the size of a backpack in your hands, held in only by a harness.

yes, he knew about cameras.

so this man at cambridge photo offered to give me all the advice i could need on all things photography. i may take him up on that. i wish i had been more interested in this when my grandfather was alive.

he didn't have many posessions when he passed away. his two biggest hobbies were photography and target shooting. i remember an occasion when i was about fourteen or so, i went with him to a target range in danvers. we had recently discovered that i have 20/10 vision ('sniper's eyes', as my doctor put it) and so my grandfather desired to see my ability with a rifle. i scored quite well, but what lingers with me today is not my ability to shoot, but my impression from firing the weapon. i remembered being afraid of the rifle. it was fairly heavy and had some kick to it. having grown up in front of computers, (which are technologically advanced and complicated devices, resulting from years of research and development) i could not comprehend how such a primitive stick, this rifle, could end human life and be the principal tool in so many conflicts.

that was the last time i fired a gun.

when my grandfather passed away i inherited all of his photography gear and my cousins, having spent most of their life in iowa, inherited the rifles.

and i had no problem with that.
