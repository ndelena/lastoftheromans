Battle of Manzikert: redux

By
Nick DeLena
on August 26, 2008 2:48 PM | Permalink | Comments (0) | TrackBacks (0)

It is fitting that Hillary Clinton, after a bitter and divisive primary battle with Barack Obama, should give her speech at the Democratic National Convention in Denver, on the 937th anniversary of the Battle of Manzikert.

Manzikert was an important trading center in ancient Armenia, which at the time was a Byzantine vassal state. The Turks, recent immigrants from Central Asia, had begun moving into lands controlled by the Roman Empire and its successor in the East, the Byzantine Empire. The Turks had no interest in being good neighbors. Motivated by Islam, they ransacked Christian Armenia and eastern Asia Minor.

The Byzantines, for many years medieval Europe's most powerful and wealthy state, had become severely weakened by a series of utterly inept leaders with no military experience - and an addiction to civil strife and palace infighting.

Events came to fruition on a warm August day in 1071 - the Turks recently conquered the Armenian capital Ani and were poised to overrun Asia Minor.

Emperor Romanus Diogenes set off with an army of 70,000 towards the East. With him, as co-regent, was Andronikos Doukas, his chief (and quite bitter) rival.

As the battle began, the Turks approached in a crescent formation, attempting to draw the Byzantines into the center to be flanked on both sides by archers. The Byzantines held off the main attack and even succeeded to seize Sultan Alp Arslan's camp by the afternoon. The Turkish cavalry, however, in classic steppe form, was harassing Byzantine forces in hit and run attacks. The Emperor, realizing the exposure of his troops, ordered his forces to pull back into a more defensive posture. Doukas deliberately ignored the Emperor's command, effectively splitting the Byzantine force in two. The Turks saw the confusion and chaos among the Byzantine troops and pressed a full attack, breaking the lines and capturing Emperor Romanos Diogenes himself. Some portion of the Byzantine army managed to escape, including much of its leadership, save for Diogenes.

While Diogenes was in captivity, his rival Doukas, returned to Constantinople and proclaimed himself Emperor.

Examined in the greater historical context of constant civil strife, within 100 years the Turks had conquered, both ethnically and religiously, nearly all of Asia Minor. Doukas' legacy is best remembered as opening the door for the final destruction of Graeco-Roman civilization in Europe - as well as the forced conversion to Islam or explusion of Greeks from what is now known as Turkey.

Clinton faces a deep personal choice tonight. Does she throw the greater good aside in her pursuit of "the purple" or does she hold ranks and keep as one for the victory of a party rival?

The road not taken

By
Nick DeLena
on April 15, 2008 9:50 AM | Permalink | Comments (0) | TrackBacks (0)
Amnesty International recently released a damming report on the death penalty worldwide for 2006 this week, noting that the U.S. has the ignoble seat at the table of the top six countries most frequently performing capital punishment. 

Here are some key facts taken from Amnesty's report:
  • 91 per cent of all known executions took place in six countries: China, Iran, Pakistan, Iraq, Sudan and the USA.
  • Some estimates say China executed at least 1,010 people, although Amnesty notes credible sources suggest that between 7,500 to 8,000 were executed.
  • Iran executed 177 people,
  • Pakistan executed 82,
  • Iraq executed at least 65,
  • Sudan executed at least 65, and
  • There were 53 executions in 12 states in the USA.
Given that no major study shows that the death penalty acts as a deterrent for crime, that all developed nations with the exceptions of South Korea and Singapore have all abolished the death penalty for this same reason - how is it we can proclaim ourselves leaders in all things humanitarian and still allow ourselves to sit in league with the most atrocious abusers of human rights on the planet? Lets separate logic from our sympathy for victims. What is the macro effect capital punishment is having on our society?

Afflicting the comfortable

By
Nick DeLena
on April 8, 2008 2:07 PM | Permalink | Comments (1) | TrackBacks (0)
Anyone else notice how closely the national press follows polls? Not coincidentally, national coverage of the Iraq war has taken a back seat to the election and the economy, which seems to be a direct reflection of national polling[1].

It's commonly said that a good journalist constantly seeks to "afflict the comfortable" - that is to bring the most troubling issues of our time front and center to the least affected. While there are plenty of good journalists scouring the world reporting on some of the most horrid issues of our time, there is a terrible haze of disinterest amongst the American public for all things unpleasant.

I don't propose this is anything new, but it seems to be more pronounced of late.



It all speaks to the cultural failure to assume personal responsibility. If you voted for Bush in 2000, or 2004, you are part responsible for this mess we're in in Iraq.

Dear American Public,

Remember when you voted for Bush (at least once), and were cheerleading the Iraq invasion? What have you been up to lately? Ignoring casualty counts? Changing the channel when the coverage of Iraq starts? Yeah, look, you can't have it both ways. Sack up, admit you made a mistake, and learn your lesson to be skeptical of leadership like the founding fathers intended.

Oh, and lose some weight you fat bastard.

Sincerely,

Last of the Romans Management.

Throwing Romney from the train

By
Nick DeLena
on December 6, 2007 9:26 PM | Permalink | Comments (0) | TrackBacks (0)
Mitt Romney gave his anticipated speech today on his Mormon faith and how that plays into his run for president. Every media outlet has been comparing it with JFK’s famous 1960 speech about Catholicism. In fact they were wildly different speeches. Romney sought to cozy up to evangelicals while Kennedy sought to distance himself from influence from the Vatican. Compare these two quotes:

Kennedy: “I believe in an America where religious intolerance will someday end; where all men and all churches are treated as equal; where every man has the same right to attend or not attend the church of his choice…”

Romney: “But in recent years, the notion of the separation of church and state has been taken by some well beyond its original meaning. They seek to remove from the public domain any acknowledgment of God. Religion is seen as merely a private affair with no place in public life. It is as if they are intent on establishing a new religion in America – the religion of secularism. They are wrong. “

Quite contrary to the spirit of the JFK speech, but the two were clearly catering to distinct interests.

Thoughts on an honest living

By
Nick DeLena
on April 6, 2006 6:36 PM | Permalink | Comments (2) | TrackBacks (0)

The erosion of American manufacturing and other "blue collar" jobs and the trend by employers in requiring college degrees across a myriad of industries has led to downward financial pressure on young men relative to their father's and grandfather's generation. At least this was the fundamental concept of a program I heard on NPR's On Point Tuesday. I thought it was pretty interesting: in my father's time it was quite possible to graduate high school and land a job that could earn you a living wage. These days, in order to do that most young Americans have to accumulate tens of thousands of dollars in college debt. At graduation they're straddled with $20,000 in college debt (national average) just to land a job with a living wage. In Massachusetts with the median home value approaching $400,000 - an entry level job here, at around $38,000 puts you in no position to buy a home. This this may have the effect of creating a class the educated but disenfranchised... an entire bloc of people unable to achieve financial stability or growth through home ownership. I think the long term effects will be troublesome.

Thermopylae, LA

By
Nick DeLena
on September 13, 2005 5:16 PM | Permalink | Comments (0) | TrackBacks (0)

I don't have a lot to say about the tragedy in New Orleans. The Economist's lead article is called "The Shaming of America" and reads something like: "The dreadful aftermath of Hurricane Katrina has exposed personal and structural failings in America's government, and eroded respect for the country around the world..." I don't go so far as that. But I do think hurricane Katrina took us face to face with our own third world: the poor, urban black South. Nearly all of the faces of the afflicted seen in disaster footage were black. Lacking the resources to escape the hurricane, they had no choice but to remain.

The aftermath, however, was truly post-apocalyptic; guns were among the first items looted at local Wal-Marts, along with televisions and electronics. Buildings were defaced. Fires set. Gangs of heavily armed young men roamed the city stealing and raping women. Nearly the entire population of the city was holed up in the superdome, with no running water, drastically low on supplies, and falling victim to the armed gangs. State and Federal officials were nowhere in sight.

It really proves that man (or maybe just American man) will almost immediately revert to barbarism and chaos once the machinations of law disappear. I've been unable to confirm it, but I heard a story of an interview with an Indonesian tsunami survivor who asked something to the effect of "Who really is the third world country? When we were hit by the tsunami we helped each other, we didn't steal and rape each other." It goes to show that wealth isn't only financial.

Also in the aftermath, many of the police of New Orleans either disappeared or failed to show up for duty, leaving only at one point about two hundred men to defend the entire city. In an interview several days later, the police chief compared their stand with that of the Greek Spartans at the Battle of Thermopylae in 480 BC. Facing an onslaught of an army forty times more numerous, the ancient Greeks used the narrow pass at Thermopylae as a way of forcing the massive Persian army into a bottleneck. However, a Greek traitor gave the Persians knowledge of an alternate path. The Greek forces, from all cities, numbering some 7,000 were sent away by their leader, Spartan king Leonidas. Interestingly, 600 Thespians under their leader Demophilus refused to leave. What is interesting is that the Thespians, unlike the militaristic Spartans, were citizen-soldiers. Demophilus made his living as an architect. The act so honored the Spartans that it is said they exchanged cloaks with the Thespians and promised to be allies for eternity. Leonidas and Demophilus remained at Thermopylae with 900 men, facing an army that could have been as large as 200,000. Though they fell, the gave enough time for the Greek army to disburse to the cities to defend them, eventually allowing the Greeks to defeat the Persians at the Battle of Salamis. And truly, in a sense the overwhelming Persian forces make a good metaphor for the floods and looting and the challenge faced by anyone who has any regard for law and order.
