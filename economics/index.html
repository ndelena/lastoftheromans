By
Nick DeLena
on April 15, 2008 10:37 AM | Permalink | Comments (0) | TrackBacks (0)
Like the dinner guest you never invited, Silvio Berlusconi has returned to office in Italy, his party winning a majority in the Italian government. Italy, as I have stated ad nauseum, is in desperate need of reform. Berlusconi is not the man for the job. While a wildly successful billionaire businessman, Berlusconi is best known in the political arena for his cafone-like outbursts that place Italy in diplomatic gaffes. Here are some of his "best" quotes:
  • "I am the Jesus Christ of politics. I am a patient victim, I put up with everyone, I sacrifice myself for everyone."
  • "I will try to meet your expectations, and I promise from now on, two-and-a-half months of absolute sexual abstinence, until [election day on] 9 April."
  • (To German MEP Martin Schulz, at start of Italy's EU presidency in July 2003):

    "I know that in Italy there is a man producing a film on Nazi concentration camps - I shall put you forward for the role of Kapo (guard chosen from among the prisoners) - you would be perfect."

  • "Let's talk about football and women." (Turning to four-times-married German Chancellor, Gerhard Schroeder.) "Gerhard, why don't you start?"
  • "Italy is now a great country to invest in... today we have fewer communists and those who are still there deny having been one. Another reason to invest in Italy is that we have beautiful secretaries... superb girls."
  • "Mussolini never killed anyone. Mussolini used to send people on vacation in internal exile."

  • "We must be aware of the superiority of our civilisation, a system that has guaranteed well-being, respect for human rights and - in contrast with Islamic countries - respect for religious and political rights, a system that has as its value understanding of diversity and tolerance...

    "The West will continue to conquer peoples, even if it means a confrontation with another civilisation, Islam, firmly entrenched where it was 1,400 years ago."

  • "There is no-one on the world stage who can compete with me."
  • (On a proposal to base an EU food standards agency in Finland, rather than the Italian city of Parma):

    "Parma is synonymous with good cuisine. The Finns don't even know what prosciutto is. I cannot accept this."

and lastly Reuters columnist Robin Pomeroy:

Italy votes, again (so soon?)

By
Nick DeLena
on April 14, 2008 9:11 AM | Permalink | Comments (1) | TrackBacks (0)
Yet another election is at hand for Italy's top spot. Italian voters have two days at the polls to decide who should take the Prime Minister's job - Walter Veltroni, center-left candidate and former Mayor of Rome, or Silvio Berlusconi, center-right candidate, twice former Prime Minister and successful businessman. Berlusconi had two chances to improve Italy's lot and did little to nothing. Italy is the slowest growing major European economy and by most accounts was just passed by Spain in per capita income. This will be Italy's 63rd government since the end of World War II.

The country needs major economic and political reforms for it to keep pace with the Eurozone. The country has one of the most regulated and bureaucratic systems of developed nations and business and proper employment struggle under the weight.

Compared with Berlusconi, Veltroni is easily my candidate of choice, however, it is very unclear whether he has the forza or intensita to make the changes that are needed. Departing Prime Minister Romano Prodi (pictured above) was hailed as the right man for the job when he took office two years ago, however, his administration was plagued by in-fighting and fractures that ultimately unwound his governing coalition. Seats in Italy's Camera dei Deputati seem to award a disproportionate share to minority and fringe parties, forcing the larger ones to make "deals with the devil" in order to form a governing coalition. While at first glance it provides a more equal voice to all perspectives, it is entirely unpractical for proper governance. 

Veltroni, some 20 years younger than Berlusconi, has taken cues from U.S. presidential candidate Barak Obama, even adopting his catch phrase, "Yes we can!"

Ma può?

Afflicting the comfortable

By
Nick DeLena
on April 8, 2008 2:07 PM | Permalink | Comments (1) | TrackBacks (0)
Anyone else notice how closely the national press follows polls? Not coincidentally, national coverage of the Iraq war has taken a back seat to the election and the economy, which seems to be a direct reflection of national polling[1].

It's commonly said that a good journalist constantly seeks to "afflict the comfortable" - that is to bring the most troubling issues of our time front and center to the least affected. While there are plenty of good journalists scouring the world reporting on some of the most horrid issues of our time, there is a terrible haze of disinterest amongst the American public for all things unpleasant.

I don't propose this is anything new, but it seems to be more pronounced of late.



It all speaks to the cultural failure to assume personal responsibility. If you voted for Bush in 2000, or 2004, you are part responsible for this mess we're in in Iraq.

Dear American Public,

Remember when you voted for Bush (at least once), and were cheerleading the Iraq invasion? What have you been up to lately? Ignoring casualty counts? Changing the channel when the coverage of Iraq starts? Yeah, look, you can't have it both ways. Sack up, admit you made a mistake, and learn your lesson to be skeptical of leadership like the founding fathers intended.

Oh, and lose some weight you fat bastard.

Sincerely,

Last of the Romans Management.

Debt Desperados

By
Nick DeLena
on May 16, 2006 11:39 PM | Permalink | Comments (1) | TrackBacks (0)

Fidelity's Stages investing newsletter profiled Jordan Goodman, author of Master Your Money Type: Using Your Financial Personality to Create a Life of Wealth and Freedom. His six "money types" are:


Strivers have energy and drive and make great entrepreneurs, but they can get into trouble when their spending and lifestyle get ahead of their earnings.
Ostriches say they're baffled by money, even while they succeed in a variety of professions. Some Ostriches are White Knighters, waiting for a "white knight" (an unexpected inheritance, for example) to rescue them from money problems.
Debt Desperados are always coming up short because of overspending or being underfinanced. These are the folks who spiral downward, creating more debt by borrowing to pay previous debt.
Coasters are organized, responsible and stable - but often too complacent, letting opportunities slip through their fingers. Although Coasters are doing fine, Goodman cautions, standing still can be riskier than they think.
High Rollers will be found gambling around a table, staking everything on a dream to build a business, or playing the stock market on margin. With proper planning, though, their energy and tenacity can win the day, according to Goodman.
Squirrels secretly fear losing everything. No matter how much they've accumulated, it isn't enough, and they risk becoming hoarders who never enjoy their wealth. "Squirrels deserve to enjoy their money now," says Goodman.

I'm pretty confident I fall under the "strivers" category... while the federal government falls under the "debt desperados" category. How amusing.

The blind leading the 20/20

By
Nick DeLena
on December 3, 2004 4:30 PM | Permalink | Comments (1) | TrackBacks (0)

This has been sitting in an Excel file on my computer since the election and I've finally had the time to make a post about it.

Further articulating the widening gulf between "blue" and "red" states, I've compiled a very topical list of socioeconomic data on all states that voted Kerry and states that voted Bush. Having them separated, I used population, median income, poverty rate and percentage of residents with a Bachelor's degree or higher. For geographic convenience I've only included the contiguous Kerry states of the northeast.

As is certainly no surprise, all of the Bush states are a statistical drag on the Kerry states and ultimately the national averages.

The Kerry states alone have an average income of $46,343 (NJ, CT, MD and MA highest, respectively with $55.1k, $53.9k, $52.8k and $50.5k), a poverty rate of 9.7% (NH, MN, and CT lowest, respectively with 6.5%, MN, CT tied at 7.9%) with 27% holding a Bachelor's degree or higher. Massachusetts leads here with 33.2%, followed closely by Maryland with 31.4%.

The Bush states have an average income of $37,259 (AL, WV, MS, and AR lowest, respectively with $24k, $29k, $31k, and $32k), a poverty rate of 13.4% (MS, LA, NM highest with a staggering 19.9%, 19.6%, 18.4% respectively), with 21.9% of the population holding Bachelor's degrees or higher. West Virginia, Mississippi and Arkansas once again occupy the lowest strata here with 14.8%, 16% and 16.7%. It should be noted that two Bush states, Colorado and Virginia had rates comparable with Kerry states (and similarly were only narrow wins for Bush).

Now instead of conveying this with a condescending tone (even though this once again proves that Massachusetts is doing it right), it very clearly shows that although defeated in the presidential election, and losing seats in congress, the Democrats have considerable opportunity in southern states. If the party once again becomes the champions of the poor and working class, while maintaining its traditional "coalition" of labor, academics and minorities, we can once again easiliy become the dominant party, as we were for the majority of the 20th century, when much progress was made.

In an interview with Ted Rall, Pittsburgh City Paper reporter Bill O'Driscoll asked what was so attractive about the right. Rall replied, "The right has been able since the Reagan revolution to express a certain kind of political dynamism that the left hasnít. The liberals have won so many battles throughout the 20th century, that by the time Reagan came along, he was able to propose ideas that were little more than unraveling those [earlier] ideas, but he was able to repackage them as revolutionary. He wanted to get rid of the progressive tax code -- itís not new, itís old,
but it looks new. People like Ann Coulter, theyíre packaging themselves as revolutionaries, and itís compelling, because you see liberals on the defensive, constantly trying to defend the old victories of civil rights, and womenís rights, the progressive tax code. And these other guys look like theyíre moving forward."

Decline and fall (of production)

By
Nick DeLena
on December 7, 2003 2:41 PM | Permalink | Comments (1) | TrackBacks (0)

Via Jim via Kottke.org via AlterNet.org: a dual book review article concerning the amount of time left before the world runs out of oil.

Marion King Hubbert, a geophysicist who spent his career in field work for Shell Oil became widely known for his model on production peaks and declines in oil production. The theory became known as "Hubbert's Peak."

Hubbert poured his own most productive years into directing research for Shell Oil, where he knew that the discovery of new U.S. oilfields had peaked in the 1930s. Hubbert factored this data into his bell-curve model, and predicted in 1956 that production of crude oil in the contiguous 48 states would peak sometime between 1966 and 1972. The oil industry dismissed his prediction and discredited his work.

U.S. crude oil production peaked in 1970, and has fallen steeply ever since.

Hubbert became a legend, and his prediction became known as "Hubbert's Peak." In the past decade ñ as the rate of discovery of worldwide oil reserves has slowed to a trickle ñ a flood of Hubbert followers have tackled the obvious question: When will global production peak?
