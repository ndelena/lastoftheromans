Thoughts on an honest living

By
Nick DeLena
on April 6, 2006 6:36 PM | Permalink | Comments (2) | TrackBacks (0)

The erosion of American manufacturing and other "blue collar" jobs and the trend by employers in requiring college degrees across a myriad of industries has led to downward financial pressure on young men relative to their father's and grandfather's generation. At least this was the fundamental concept of a program I heard on NPR's On Point Tuesday. I thought it was pretty interesting: in my father's time it was quite possible to graduate high school and land a job that could earn you a living wage. These days, in order to do that most young Americans have to accumulate tens of thousands of dollars in college debt. At graduation they're straddled with $20,000 in college debt (national average) just to land a job with a living wage. In Massachusetts with the median home value approaching $400,000 - an entry level job here, at around $38,000 puts you in no position to buy a home. This this may have the effect of creating a class the educated but disenfranchised... an entire bloc of people unable to achieve financial stability or growth through home ownership. I think the long term effects will be troublesome.

By J.Cormier on April 10, 2006 10:59 AM

This is indeed troubling; even more troubling is the increasing worthlessness of an undergraduate degree. Particularly given the seeming proliferation of college curricula which stress "well-roundedness". The irony is that the more well-rounded you are as a college student, the less useful you are to any potential employers. It seems like you have to go to business school or choose one of the dwindling list of majors/concentrations that lead to legitimate post-college careers in order to succeed with only a bachelor's degree. For instance, business school graduates or people with majors like journalism or degrees in science probably won't have too much trouble finding jobs, but those who concentrate in areas not directly suitable to the market are out of luck. Everything seems to require higher levels of education; even the ones that might land you a job straight out don't necessarily lead to anything fruitful until you eventually get a higher degree (i.e., business, journalism, the sciences).

It's depressing. Particularly when you see people behind companies like Google and other crazy startups that really didn't have to finish college. I've been telling my brother, who is starting to look at schools now, not to underestimate any of his hobbies or interests. Whether you're into silk-screening T-shirts or building up and selling characters on World of Warcraft, you never know when it might make you a living, if not a million.
