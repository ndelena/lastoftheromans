la Città Eterna

By
Nick DeLena
on April 21, 2006 5:21 PM | Permalink | Comments (1) | TrackBacks (0)

Today marks Rome, the eternal city's 2,759th birthday. The city's founding is shrouded in myth, but legend has it the city was founded by the orphaned brothers Romulus and Remus, after being nursed in infancy by a she-wolf. Eventually the two brothers fought over where the city should be founded, and Romulus slew Remus and founded Rome on the Palatine Hill.

Rome's contributions to European and ultimately human civilization are immeasurable. As Rome's influence spread, it found a sparsely populated, barbaric Europe and left it networked with roads, bridges, cities of brick and marble, endowed with language, arts, crafts and laws.

At its height the Roman Empire spanned an area greater than the United States and encompassed anywhere from 21% to 40% of the world's population. It's realm stretched from the cold and desolate Scottish border, through continental Europe, into the middle east as far as the Persian Gulf. It included all of present day England, France, Portugal, Spain, Belgium, Switzerland, Italy, Slovenia, Yugoslavia, Romania, Bulgaria, Greece, Turkey, Armenia, Cyprus, Syria, Israel, Palestine, Lebanon, Jordan, Iraq, and included the majority of Austria, Morocco, Algeria, the Netherlands, Libya, Egypt, and the Czech Republic. One could not land on any shore of the Mediterranean and escape Roman arms. It's legacy can still be seen in the immensity of its architecture spanning then what was nearly the entirety of the known world.

Indeed the limits of Rome's power were seen as endless, as Josephus said as he pleaded with Judeans to end their rebellion against Rome's rule: "That certainly they have seen their strongest walls demolished, and that the wall still remaining was weaker than those that were already taken. That they must know the Roman power was invincible, and that they had been used to serve them; for, that in case it be allowed a right thing to fight for liberty, that ought to have been done at first; but for them that have once fallen under the power of the Romans, and have now submitted to them for so many long years, to pretend to shake off that yoke afterward, was the work of such as had a mind to die miserably, not of such as were lovers of liberty. Besides, men may well enough grudge at the dishonor of owning ignoble masters over them, but ought not to do so to those who have all things under their command; for what part of the world is there that hath escaped the Romans, unless it be such as are of no use for violent heat, or for violent cold? And evident it is that fortune is on all hands gone over to them; and that God, when he had gone round the nations with this dominion, is now settled in Italy."

Rome's population reached its peak in the 2nd century AD at 2,000,000 persons. It did not reach this level again until the 20th century AD. It is no coincidence that the fall of the Roman Empire marked the start of the dark ages.

Happy 2,759th, Roma.

Italian opposition party wins election

By
Nick DeLena
on April 11, 2006 10:03 AM | Permalink | Comments (0) | TrackBacks (0)

Romano Prodi, head of the center-left coalition L'Unione has won Italian elections by a razor-thin margin over incumbent Prime Minister Silvio Berlusconi's Forza Italia party.

From Yahoo! News:

"Final returns showed Prodi winning the lower Chamber of Deputies by one-tenth of a percentage point: 49.8 to 49.7 percent. Under Italian electoral law, 55 percent of seats are awarded to the overall winner regardless of the scale of victory, giving Prodi's forces at least 340 seats in the 630-member lower house.

According to official returns, Berlusconi's conservative allies held a one-seat advantage in the Senate, with 155 seats to Prodi's 154. But with some 852 overseas precincts out of 896 reporting, returns indicated Prodi's forces would capture another four seats, while one seat would go to Berlusconi's allies and one to an independent party, giving Prodi the majority."
This is yet another country with pro-Bush leadership to flip sides as the fallout of Bush's foreign policy and the Iraq debackle spreads further and further. We saw elections in South America result in victories for the center-left for nearly all of the southern nations.

Berlusconi's coalition allies are the former neo-fascist National Alliance, pro-Vatican forces and the anti-immigrant Northern League. Prodi's are moderate Christian Democrats, Greens, liberals, Communists and former Communists.

You gotta love Italian politics. I'll be on top of this one.

Thoughts on an honest living

By
Nick DeLena
on April 6, 2006 6:36 PM | Permalink | Comments (2) | TrackBacks (0)

The erosion of American manufacturing and other "blue collar" jobs and the trend by employers in requiring college degrees across a myriad of industries has led to downward financial pressure on young men relative to their father's and grandfather's generation. At least this was the fundamental concept of a program I heard on NPR's On Point Tuesday. I thought it was pretty interesting: in my father's time it was quite possible to graduate high school and land a job that could earn you a living wage. These days, in order to do that most young Americans have to accumulate tens of thousands of dollars in college debt. At graduation they're straddled with $20,000 in college debt (national average) just to land a job with a living wage. In Massachusetts with the median home value approaching $400,000 - an entry level job here, at around $38,000 puts you in no position to buy a home. This this may have the effect of creating a class the educated but disenfranchised... an entire bloc of people unable to achieve financial stability or growth through home ownership. I think the long term effects will be troublesome.
