The old country

By
Nick DeLena
on August 19, 2005 5:21 PM | Permalink | Comments (0) | TrackBacks (0)

Back in September of 2003 I made a post here called "Fambrosium" where I mentioned how I looked up my family's information from the Ellis Island website. I had written a bit about my family and their town, Afragola, in Campania. In the comments about the post I mentioned that in a travel guide I own of Italy, Afragola is mentioned as falling within a so called "triangle of death" involving the local mafia.

Not long after that, I discovered a post on an Italian message board, written by an Afragoleso apparently chiding me for insulting Afragola. He wrote this long tirade in Italian about me, my site and my friends who commented. My original post about that is here:"Un mare di risate.".

Interestingly, last month while Kristen and I were in Italy, we took a trip to Campania, to Naples, Pompeii and Positano on the Amalfi coast. We (mostly I) made the near-fatal mistake of forgetting to check the time of the last train of the night departing Naples for Rome and we ended up missing it by an hour and a half.

So there we were, 11pm in Naples with the next train not leaving until 4:50am. We were without passports so we couldn't rent a room for the night. For those of you who haven't been to Naples, it isn't exactly the most friendly city. It's the pickpocket capital of Europe, perhaps the world, and the area of the train station is filled with the homeless, drug addicts, vagrants and lots and lots of bony dogs that roam the streets. I had a strict policy of looking Italian that week - not wearing shorts, undershirts, polo shirts, or sneakers and instead wearing tight fitting jeans, a buttoned shirt opened down a few buttons, and shoes. As Italian as we both are, and as we both looked, we still got eyed from sketchy people lurking in the shadows of the station. I had my brand new Canon ZR300 digital video camera slung in a generic black bag over my shoulder. Kristen had her relatively new Olympia digital still camera in her bag with about 200 pictures on it. So we were concerned.

After about forty five minutes of unsuccessfully searching for departing trains and accomodations, we were resigned to the fact that we would have to spend the next five hours huddled up in a defensive mound of person, one person sleeping at a time. Just as we had realized this, a vaguely suspicious man comes up and sees Kristen looking for a sign for the bathroom.

"Where you go?"
"I'm looking for the Bathroom."
"Rome! You going to Rome?"
"Well..."
"Ok. You go to Rome? You listen..."

He then told us how the train station in Naples was closed for the night but in Caserta, about 25km north, a train passes through at 1am stopping in Rome by 4am on its way to Milan and that he could take us there. The pricing scheme was elaborate at best. €2 per km within Naples, double that outside the city plus unnamed fees. Given our options we reluctantly accepted.

He took us away from the main entrance of the station, through what seemed to be an industrial service corridor. Kristen, rightfully, was concerned about where he was taking us. Eventually we got to the back entrance and the parking lot. He led us to a generic car that had some taxi insignias, but no meter. I told him that I wasn't sure how legitimate it was. He insisted that he was a "registered service car of the station" and showed me some paperwork. A few seconds later one of the uniformed parking lot attendants came over to vouch for his friend's legitimacy. We grudgingly accepted - and his friend came along for the ride, which we found suspicious. The man drove extremely fast and for a while we couldn't tell if he was actually taking us to Caserta. Kristen hid her camera's memory cards.

Eventually we were able to tell that we were going the correct way. About ten minutes outside of Naples I saw signs for Afragola. But that was about all I saw of it. After about fifteen more minutes we got to Caserta.

And that's when it all made sense.

€200.

He wasn't going to steal our wallets and leave us. He didn't need to. He drove us 25km and charged us €200... we had no choice but to pay.

So that sucked. But ultimately to make ourselves feel better we reasoned that it was better than waiting five ours in the train station at Naples.

So... to my friend who posted on the bulletin board... you've come a long way... but there's still room for improvement.

Sic transit gloria

By
Nick DeLena
on August 11, 2005 7:22 PM | Permalink | Comments (0) | TrackBacks (0)

I'm really trying to catch this site up on things. I left Revere City Hall in July for a much better job with Vitale, Caturano & Company as a risk consultant. I started on July 25. During the week before that, I was in Italy with Kristen. Five days in Rome and one day in Pompeii and the Amalfi coast (Positano). It of course was absolutely incredible. I have pictures up here. Work has been pretty busy, I spent a total of three days in the office before they shuttled me off to a client where I've been helping end a project. Now it looks like next week I'll be working with a pharmaceutical firm.

The company offers a tuition reimbursement program for "work related academics." I don't know if I can get reimbursed for a Public Administration/Political Science curriculum by a CPA/Business consulting firm. I've been investigating other masters degree options that would represent a middle ground. I've found one so far - a gMBA - or global MBA - a business degree that focuses heavily on international issues. I could justify that one, as several of my coworkers were sent abroad for business, and I may be too. It's something to think about. Suggestions are appreciated.

You may have noticed some small, grainy pictures have been posted here. I bought a new cell phone - a Treo 650 Smartphone with a camera/camcorder and the ability to take a picture and have it posted here almost immediately, a very unnecessary yet equally entertaining feature. So don't be surprised if you see random pictures pop up here from my travels.

No explanation for the included picture will be given.

Exeunt.
