Rice confirmation shows Dem split

By
Nick DeLena
on January 30, 2005 2:20 PM | Permalink | Comments (16) | TrackBacks (0)


Howard Dean's (L) beliefs for the Democratic party differ significantly with Senate Minority Leader Harry Reid's (at right).

As Condoleeza Rice's senate confirmation hearings got underway, the ideological divisions within the Democratic party shone through. During the first day, in front of the Senate Foreign Relations Committee, Senator Barbara Boxer (D-California) was the most aggressive, saying Rice's loyalty to President Bush "overwhelmed [her] respect for the truth." Rice also declined to answer Democrats' questions as to if she believed extreme interrogation tactics amounted to torture. The only notable challenges to Rice came from Boxer and from Sen. John Kerry. I was particularly disappointed by Barak Obama (D-Illinois) and his failure to mount what could have been a very articulate criticism.

Eventually the committee voted 16-2 to approve her. The only Democrats who voted against her were Boxer and Kerry. It seems that the majority of Democrats on the committee, even with their questions, were resigned to a sense of self-fulfilling expectancy.

For years now there have been two schools of thought in the Democratic party, one believes that the only way to win elections is to soften core beliefs on abortion, gay marriage, taxes, social security, etc., etc., until the party becomes something like Diet Republican. The other school of thought - and the school of thought I subscribe to - holds that the Democrats' problem has not been it's core values, but rather the way it has marketed itself. Republicans have implemented a 30 year plan to take over talk radio, news shows, news columns, and to create a bond with the "common man" even though Republican policies generally benefit the uber-wealthy. My school of thought holds that the Democrats must stand their ground, reshape their image and fight harder for the values and ideas that have driven the better parts of the 20th century. Democrats must stand up and fight against the privatization of social security, the increasing radicalization of America, and a disasterous foreign policy that has undermined us at every step.

The election of Howard Dean to the chair of the DNC would stand in stark contrast to the election of Harry Reid (D-Nevada) as Senate Minority Leader. Reid is somewhat of a Diet Republican and symbolic of the failure of Democrats to "firm up" their image and their beliefs. He has a very vague opposition to abortion and seems to meager to lead the party in the legislature. We know he opposes social security privatization. But do we know where he stands on other issues? (Not really.)

Dean on the other hand - like him or not - you know where he stands (much like George the W). During his campaign he vowed to win the support of the Southern working class (a 50 state campaign as opposed to the traditional 18 state campaign the Dems usually run). He would, if nothing else, cement the party to its base and hopefully give it a makeover the likes of which we have not seen since FDR's election in 1932 (and '36 and '40 and '44).
